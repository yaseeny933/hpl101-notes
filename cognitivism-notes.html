01 The core idea

Cognitivism arose in the mid-20th century partly as a reaction against behaviorism. It keeps the idea that the environment (instruction, demonstration) shapes learning — but flatly rejects the "black box."

To a cognitivist, the mind is absolutely not a black box. What matters is exactly what behaviorism ignored: the concepts inside the learner's head, and the practices (a good analogy, the right example) that get the learner to reorganize or change those concepts. Learning = changing mental representations and structures.

03 Schemas — knowledge as a network

Existing knowledge lives in schemas: organized systems of information, ideas, meanings, conditions, and — crucially — the relationships among them. Learning means changing or elaborating a schema, not just adding an isolated fact. Instructional support and feedback help the learner make the right connections.

A schema is a web of related concepts. New learning = wiring a new node into the existing web.

04 Two ways a schema changes

When new experience arrives, the mind does one of two things (Piaget's terms, from the schema video):

Assimilation fits in

New information slots into an existing schema without changing it. The framework already accounts for it.

e.g. A student who understands the internment as unjust easily files a new wartime example under the same "injustice" schema.

Accommodation reshapes

New information doesn't fit, so the schema itself must be revised or rebuilt to make sense of it.

e.g. Realizing "national security" was used to justify a racist injustice forces a learner to rebuild what that phrase means to them.

05 Intuitive theories

Beyond schemas, learners hold intuitive theories — abstract, causal frameworks they use to predict, explain, reason about, and intervene on the world, much like scientists build theories of nature.

The surprising finding: knowledge does not simply go concrete → abstract. Even very young children form abstract causal theories first — sometimes before the concrete facts.
Goals before customsInfants expect agents to pursue goals via the easiest, most direct path — before they learn specific customs like handshakes or bowing.
Rules before exceptionsToddlers grasp abstract syntax (verb conjugation, word order) before memorizing irregulars — hence "I goed" instead of "I went."
Kids ask "why?"Frank Keil (Wonder): young children constantly ask explanatory "why" questions and can reason about causal mechanisms.
Don't underestimate themCurricula that delay causal explanation to grades 6–8 underestimate children and miss the chance to support natural inquiry.

↳ Teaching takeaway: give learners abstract, explanatory frameworks early, and encourage causal questioning — it both motivates and supports learning.

06 Nyan's lesson, decoded

His revised plan drops reinforcement and instead works on students' mental structures. Each idea maps to a cognitivist mechanism:

Lead a discussion where students surface their existing ideas and beliefs about when imprisoning people is just or unjust.

Activate prior schemas Surfaces what's already in the learner's head so new content can connect to it — learning starts from existing concepts and beliefs.

Later, ask whether their understanding of the internment applies to the U.S. creation of Native American "reservations" and theft of tribal lands.

Accommodation Forces students to test a schema against a new case — stretching, elaborating, or rebuilding it (and transferring across contexts).

Have students create concept maps and diagrams to identify and visualize connections among concepts.

Externalize the schema Concept maps make the web of relationships visible — the very structure cognitivism says learning reorganizes.

07 Glossary — quiz yourself first

Try to define each term before you reveal it.

schemas
Organized systems of information, ideas, meanings, conditions, and the relationships among them — the mental structures that hold existing knowledge. Learning happens by changing or elaborating them. Think: a web of connected concept-nodes, not a list of facts.
cognitivism
A theory of learning, emerging mid-20th century in response to behaviorism, that focuses on how information is received, organized, stored, and retrieved by the mind. The learner is active, and the mind is not a black box. Behaviorism asks "what response?" — cognitivism asks "what changed in the mind?"
intuitive theory
An abstract, causal framework a person uses to predict, explain, reason about, and intervene on the world — much like a scientific theory. Even very young children form these, sometimes before concrete knowledge. "I goed" — the child applies an abstract rule before learning the irregular exception.

★ Remember this

  • Open the box. Cognitivism studies what's inside the mind — the thing behaviorism deliberately ignored.
  • Learning = changing mental structures, not building stimulus-response habits.
  • Schemas are webs of related concepts; you learn by wiring new nodes in (assimilation) or rebuilding the web (accommodation).
  • The learner is active — planning, goal-setting, rehearsing, relating new info to prior knowledge.
  • Intuitive theories come early. Kids reason causally and abstractly young — don't underestimate them.
