Identities & Learning

Identity is never just personal. Others attribute identities to learners, learners internalize them — and schooling can either wound those identities or sustain them.

attributed identity stereotype threat culturally sustaining pedagogy ethnic studies

01 The core idea

A secure identity is a resource for learning — but identity is shaped by outside forces. Two of them dominate this sequence: identities attributed to learners by others, and the harmful expectations learners come to internalize about themselves.

This sequence moves from harm to repair. First it names how bias and the "hidden curriculum" damage learners with marginalized identities (4.3.1–4.3.2). Then it turns to asset-based responses — culturally responsive, relevant, and sustaining education, and ethnic studies — that treat learners' cultures and histories as "precious knowledge" rather than deficits (4.3.3–4.3.4).

Attributed identities can become internalized — and self-fulfilling. Asset-based pedagogy is the intervention that breaks the loop.

02 4.3.1 — Attributed learner identities

An attributed identity is one that others perceive and project onto a learner. Even a learner with a secure identity stays susceptible to harm when others see them through a negative or stereotyped lens — e.g., as "aggressive" or a "troublemaker."

Disproportionate discipline

  • Subjective vs. objective rule-breaking. Huang & Cornell (2017): across 50,000+ Virginia students, Black students were ~3× more likely than White students to be suspended — driven by subjective infractions (arguing, "bad language," talking too much). For objective violations (weapons, drugs), there were no disparities or higher White rates.
  • It starts in preschool. Gilliam (2016, Yale): teachers shown a video spent the most time monitoring the Black boy for "challenging behavior" — and this held for both Black and White teachers.
  • The ethnic discipline gap. Nguyen et al. (2019): lumping all groups into "Asian American & Pacific Islander" hides that Cambodian, Lao, and Pacific Islander youth are disciplined far more than East Asian or White students. Disaggregating the data reveals it.
  • Adultification bias. Adults view Black girls as more mature and less in need of nurturing, protection, and comfort (Blake & Epstein, 2019) — so they're punished more harshly and in developmentally inappropriate ways.
  • Disability, too. Learners with disabilities are ~8.6% of public-school students but 32% of youth in juvenile detention (Elias, 2013).

Harsh discipline removes learners from class, erodes belonging and motivation, and feeds the school-to-prison pipeline — funneling students out of school and toward the juvenile-justice system.

Implicit bias

We're socialized to carry implicit associations — negative ones about marginalized groups, positive ones about dominant groups — outside our conscious awareness. Even members of stigmatized groups absorb some of these, and even people committed to equality can show automatic preferences for dominant groups. The Implicit Association Test (IAT) (Banaji and colleagues) measures bias best in the aggregate; a single result doesn't diagnose an individual, but it's useful for self-reflection. Bias is only one strand of a larger web of cognitive, organizational, economic, and political structures.

03 4.3.2 — Internalized learner identities

If 4.3.1 is what others attribute, 4.3.2 is what learners come to internalize — the societal assumptions about their group that they implicitly accept about themselves.

The hidden curriculum

Beyond the explicit, planned curriculum lies the hidden curriculum: the unwritten, sometimes harmful norms, values, and behaviors learners absorb in educational settings. Sociologists (from the 1960s on) showed schooling quietly teaches docility and compliance — the teacher as a child's "first boss" (Jackson, 1968) — and that practices like tracking habituate learners to social stratification, making inequality feel "natural."

The hidden curriculum doesn't occur in a vacuum — it tends to reinforce dominant narratives of power.

Examples: anglicizing East/South Asian students' names (signaling the dominant group's comfort matters more than students' identities); science texts that erase everyone but White men; spotlighting only "exceptional" women (Curie, Franklin), implying women must be extraordinary to belong — and that the system itself needn't change.

Imposter syndrome

Imposter syndrome is an overwhelming subjective sense of incompetence — common among highly successful people ("I got in by luck," "everyone here knows more than I do"). It's intractable because the feeling persists even when the person can logically see the achievement was earned. Crucially, it's not the student's sole responsibility: institutions create it through unwritten rules, so schools and workplaces must counteract the hidden curriculum that fuels it.

Stereotype threat

Steele & Aronson (1995): African-American and White students with equal SAT scores were told a test measured "reading and verbal reasoning ability." Under that framing the African-American students underperformed. Women similarly underperformed in math when told the test measured "mathematical ability."

  • It's a self-fulfilling prophecy. Awareness of a stereotype about "people like me" can throw learners "off their game" (Spencer, Steele & Quinn, 1999) — crossing out correct answers, avoiding hard problems.
  • It eats working memory. The anxiety of the threat condition consumes mental bandwidth needed to solve problems (Schmader & Johns, 2003). The effort not to confirm the stereotype ends up confirming it.
  • It's worst under judgment + salience. Strongest when one is being evaluated and is a visible minority in the room (Good, Aronson & Inzlicht, 2003).

Countermeasures: (1) Manipulate test instructions — telling women a test was designed to eliminate gender differences erased the gap; (2) Invoke a growth mindset — teaching that intelligence is malleable and that struggle is normal in a new context improved math achievement and closed the gender gap.

↳ Note: a more inclusive climate raises belonging but does not by itself eliminate stereotype threat — the sense of being judged as a representative of one's group can remain. Undocumented students are a stark case: exclusion burdens them not only with inequity but with internalized anxiety and shame.

04 4.3.3 — Culturally responsive, relevant & sustaining education

Every learning environment encodes a belief system about what counts as knowledge. Those values may not match the "deep culture" a learner brings. Asset-based pedagogies treat that culture — students' funds of knowledge and cultural wealth (Yosso, 2005) — as entry points, not deficits.

The values embedded in a learning environment may not align with the "deep culture" from which learners derive their own beliefs and values.

CRRE — Ladson-Billings' three tenets

Academic success tenet 1

High expectations for all learners, plus the tools and supports to reach them.

Cultural competence tenet 2

Build on students' prior knowledge and cultural backgrounds, while helping them navigate systems that marginalize some groups.

Critical consciousness tenet 3

Develop learners' ability to recognize and critique the status-quo systems that produce social inequity.

CRRE = "culturally responsive and relevant education" — a half-century of work helping students connect cultural identity to academic success, promoting learning, equity, and social justice. In practice: Maung Ting Nyeu has CHT (Bangladesh) children collect elders' traditional stories and turn them into bilingual books — oral tradition becomes a bridge to literacy in the societal language. Studies link CRRE to better test scores, motivation, and perspective-taking (e.g., using math to analyze racial profiling in traffic-stop data).

The progression: responsive → relevant → sustaining

CSP is a "loving critique" of CRRE — it goes beyond including cultures to sustaining them.

Culturally sustaining pedagogy (CSP)

Django Paris (2012) — with H. Samy Alim (2017) — calls CSP a "loving critique" of earlier asset-based pedagogies. CSP centers and sustains students' languages, literacies, and ways of being, honoring how youth dynamically rework their cultures today. It more explicitly critiques oppressive systems and aims at social transformation toward cultural pluralism, not mere inclusion.

05 4.3.4 — Ethnic studies as "precious knowledge"

Ethnic studies is both a scholarly field and a K–12 subject. It offers a critical perspective on curricula long centered on dominant groups (in the U.S., White European-Americans), teaching a counter-narrative that builds critical consciousness about power and privilege — using an antiracist, relational pedagogy that recovers dehumanized histories.

Case study — Tucson's Mexican-American Studies (MAS) program

The Mexican-American Studies (MAS) program in Tucson, Arizona (also called la Raza Studies) taught Indigenous Xicanx intellectual traditions, art, and literature — knowledge students and teachers called "precious knowledge."

The program was legally banned, then reinstated after court battles over whether it was "exclusionary" or a protected exercise of free speech. Student Mariah Harvey describes how an ethnic-studies course reshaped her understanding of her community and of her own identity.

Deeper roots: educators have built counter-narratives for as long as U.S. schools have existed — e.g., "Negro History Week" in the 1930s (Givens, 2019). Angela Davis recalled teachers urging students to set aside racist textbooks and "rely on our own ability to produce knowledge about the conditions surrounding our lives."

What's the evidence?

Dee & Penner (2017) used a natural experiment in San Francisco (SFUSD): students just below a GPA cutoff were assigned to an ethnic-studies course; those just above were a near-identical comparison group. Among the assigned students, the course:

+21percentage points in attendance (fewer unexcused absences)
+1.4grade points in 9th-grade GPA (on a 4.0 scale)
+23credits earned (about four courses)

A well-implemented ethnic-studies course can substantially improve learning outcomes.

Take a critical stance

Does this mean teaching learners in culturally homogeneous groups? Not quite. Module 3 warned against deficit-oriented grouping like tracking. But affinity grouping — based on shared identity or goals — can be beneficial when the purpose is intragroup relationship-building, dialogue, and action, especially around marginalized identities. Think HBCUs, Aboriginal community schools, single-sex girls' schools, or Gallaudet University.

06 Glossary — quiz yourself first

Try to define each term before you reveal it.

attributed identity
An identity that others perceive and project onto a learner — independent of how the learner sees themselves. Negative attributions (e.g., "aggressive," "troublemaker") leave learners vulnerable to harm even when their own identity is secure. A teacher reads a Black boy's behavior as "challenging" before he's done anything notable.
adultification bias
Viewing Black girls and youth as more mature and less in need of nurturing, protection, support, and comfort than they actually are — leading to unfair, developmentally inappropriate treatment.
school-to-prison pipeline
A phenomenon by which harsh school discipline increases the likelihood of dropping out and entering the juvenile-justice system — funneling students out of school and into the correctional system.
implicit bias
Automatic, often unconscious associations — negative about marginalized groups, positive about dominant ones — that shape our reactions outside direct awareness. Even members of stigmatized groups and people committed to equality can hold them. Best measured in the aggregate (e.g., via the IAT), not as an individual diagnosis.
internalized expectations
Societal assumptions tied to particular groups or social identities that a learner comes to implicitly accept or believe about themselves.
hidden curriculum
The unwritten, sometimes unintended (and sometimes harmful) norms, values, and behaviors that students learn in educational settings — beyond the explicit, planned curriculum. Tracking quietly teaches that society is divided into classes; anglicizing names signals whose comfort matters.
social stratification
A sociology term for the relative social position of individuals across different groups (strata). The hidden curriculum can naturalize it.
imposter syndrome
A sometimes-overwhelming subjective sense of incompetence — common among highly successful people. Often produced by institutions' unwritten rules, so overcoming it is not the student's responsibility alone.
stereotype threat
Diminished performance by members of a group when that group's social identity is made salient in a learning situation and is associated with implicit negative assumptions about their academic ability. Equally-scoring students underperform when a test is framed as measuring their group's stereotyped weakness.
culturally responsive & relevant education (CRRE)
Instruction that acknowledges and builds on the varied cultural resources of learners — connecting cultural identity to academic success. Ladson-Billings' three tenets: academic success, cultural competence, critical consciousness.
culturally sustaining pedagogy (CSP)
Instruction that centers and seeks to foster the languages, literacies, and ways of being of students' communities toward cultural pluralism — going beyond inclusion to actively sustain living, shifting cultures (Paris, 2012).
funds of knowledge / cultural wealth
The domains of knowledge and assets learners bring from their own (and multiple) cultural contexts (Yosso, 2005). Acknowledging them provides entry points for learning and reinforces resilience.
critical consciousness
A learner's developed ability to recognize and critique the status-quo systems and norms that produce and maintain social inequity.
ethnic studies
A field of scholarship — and a political and social movement — offering a critical perspective on traditional curricula focused on dominant subgroups. It recovers a historical narrative that promotes critical consciousness about power and privilege, reversing dehumanizing histories through an antiracist, relational pedagogy.
affinity grouping
Grouping based on shared social identities and/or goals to support intragroup relationship-building, dialogue, and action — distinct from deficit-oriented grouping like tracking.

07 Practice & discussion

Answer in your head (or on paper) before revealing. Mix of recall and applied scenarios.

recallIn Huang & Cornell's Virginia study, for which kind of infraction did racial disparities in suspension appear — and which not?
Disparities appeared for subjective infractions identified by an authority figure — arguing with a teacher, "bad language," talking excessively. For objective, clear-cut violations (weapons, smoking, drugs) there were either no racial disparities or a higher rate among White students. The gap lives in interpretation, not in objectively worse behavior.
applyA 10th-grader who aces her homework freezes and underperforms on a high-stakes math exam where she's one of few girls. Name the phenomenon and two evidence-based ways the teacher could reduce it.
This is stereotype threat — strongest under evaluation + identity salience (being a visible minority). Two countermeasures: (1) manipulate the framing/instructions — e.g., state the test was designed to eliminate gender differences, or that it's not diagnostic of ability; (2) invoke a growth mindset — teach that intelligence is malleable and that struggle in a hard, new context is normal, not a sign of deficiency. Both have closed gender gaps in math performance.
recallWhat is the "hidden curriculum," and give one concrete example from the unit.
The hidden curriculum is the unwritten, sometimes harmful norms, values, and behaviors students absorb in educational settings, beyond the explicit lesson plan. Examples: tracking habituating students to social stratification; anglicizing Asian students' names; science texts that omit everyone but White men; spotlighting only "exceptional" women, implying women must be extraordinary to belong.
applyA district wants to add an ethnic-studies elective but the school board doubts it will help "real" academics. What evidence from Dee & Penner (2017) would you cite, and why is the study persuasive?
In SFUSD, students assigned to ethnic studies (just below a GPA cutoff) saw attendance rise +21 percentage points, ninth-grade GPA rise +1.4 points, and credits earned rise by ~23 (≈ four courses) versus a near-identical group just above the cutoff. It's persuasive because the cutoff design compares students who were academically very similar, supporting a causal interpretation rather than mere correlation.
applyA colleague says, "If we want to honor students' cultures, isn't culturally responsive teaching enough? Why bother with 'sustaining'?" How would you distinguish CRRE from CSP?
CRRE acknowledges and builds on students' cultural resources to connect identity to academic success. CSP (Paris, 2012) is a "loving critique" that goes further: it seeks to foster and sustain students' languages, literacies, and ways of being — treating cultures as living and dynamically reworked by youth — and more explicitly critiques oppressive systems, aiming at social transformation toward cultural pluralism. In short: responsive/relevant includes culture; sustaining actively perpetuates it.

Open-ended discussion prompts

  1. Where have you experienced (or enacted) a hidden curriculum — and whose comfort or norms did it quietly center? What would it take to make those norms explicit?
  2. The IAT can't diagnose an individual's bias. Given that, what is the responsible use of implicit-bias awareness in teacher preparation — and where could it do harm if misused?
  3. Module 3 warned against deficit-oriented grouping, yet affinity grouping can support marginalized learners. How do you tell the two apart in practice, and what purpose makes affinity grouping defensible?

★ Remember this

  • Attributed → internalized. Identities others project onto learners (bias, low expectations, disproportionate discipline) can be absorbed and become self-fulfilling.
  • Discipline gaps are about interpretation. Disparities show up for subjective infractions, not objective ones — and surface as early as preschool, across teacher races.
  • The hidden curriculum teaches the unspoken — norms that often reinforce dominant power and breed imposter syndrome; institutions, not just students, must counteract it.
  • Stereotype threat hits under judgment + identity salience and eats working memory — but framing changes and a growth mindset can shrink or erase the gap.
  • Asset-based pedagogy breaks the loop. CRRE builds on culture (academic success, cultural competence, critical consciousness); CSP goes further to sustain living cultures.
  • Ethnic studies is "precious knowledge" — Tucson's MAS was banned then reinstated, and SFUSD evidence shows real gains in attendance, GPA, and credits.
