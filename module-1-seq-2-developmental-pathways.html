Developmental Pathways

Development follows generalized trajectories — common in order, loose in timing — and our theories only count as universal if the evidence reaches beyond a narrow slice of humanity.

developmental trajectory perspective-taking stage theories WEIRD critique

01 The core idea

Children develop in a recognizable progression — most point before they label, babble "ba" and "ga" before words. But skills don't lock in on a strict timetable. There's usually a long window in which a capacity may emerge, shaped by many factors.

A developmental trajectory is a generalized timeline for the emergence of successively more complex skills and performances. It lets us name what's common across typically developing learners while acknowledging that individual differences and experience influence each learner's rate of growth and ultimate capacities.

Common in order, variable in timing — individuals conform more or less strictly to the general pattern.

This sequence uses one domain — social perspective-taking (how people come to understand others' beliefs and intentions) — as a concrete case study of what a trajectory looks like, then zooms out to stage theories and to who developmental science has actually studied.

02 1.2.1 · Developmental Trajectories

Some skills emerge at about the same age for most children worldwide, regardless of geography or environment. Others emerge in a predictable order but not a predictable age. A trajectory captures both regularities while leaving room for individual variation.

Example of variation: while hearing children learn to distinguish the sounds of their native language, deaf children instead develop much more elaborate gestural communication. Individual differences can accelerate development of some capacities.

Practitioners and theorists approach the same concept with different questions:

The educator / practitioner apply

  • When is a learner showing normal vs. delayed development of a skill?
  • How can we best support the emergence and ongoing development of key skills?
  • What steps do we take if learners aren't making expected progress?

The developmental theorist explain

  • When does a skill tend to emerge — and why not earlier?
  • How do environmental forces influence the timing, speed, and ease of development?
  • What are the constraints on the order and speed of development?
A domain of development is an area of capability that people can develop over the life course, from infancy into adulthood — an area in which people grow in some capacity to engage with the world. (We'll revisit "domains" later in the course.)

03 1.2.2 · Development in Social Perspective-Taking

The ability to attribute mental states to ourselves and others — to infer people's beliefs, thoughts, feelings, and intentions — is theory of mind, and it's essential for navigating social life. Crucially, people are not born with it: young children can be genuinely confused about what others think.

The Holly Dilemma — eliciting the trajectory

A task created by HGSE Professor Robert Selman to elicit children's thinking about moral issues. Holly has promised her father she won't climb trees, but her friend's kitten is stuck up one. Researchers presented the dilemma to many children of different ages and backgrounds, then looked across the aggregated responses to find meaningful age-to-age differences — and from that theorized a typical developmental trajectory for social perspective-taking.

What does (and doesn't) count as theory of mind?

The check-for-understanding items hinge on one distinction: theory of mind is about recognizing that another person's mental states differ from your own — not about obedience, moral ranking, or noticing danger.

04 1.2.3 · Domains & Stage Theories

Do trajectories in any two domains have unique "fingerprints," or shared commonalities? One way to look for commonalities is to seek domain-general changes — similarities in the complexity of abilities displayed across specific domains.

Piaget's stages — and their cracks

Swiss psychologist Jean Piaget proposed sequential stages in children's cognitive abilities, seeing them as the product of the child's autonomous actions in the world — de-emphasizing instruction or engineering of the social environment. But children's behavior was never as cleanly delineated as the theory required. Piaget coined "décalage" for this blurriness — uncorrelated, inconsistent stage transitions.

Neo-PiagetiansSuccessors (incl. HGSE's Kurt Fischer) reinterpreted "blurriness": inconsistent performance can be evidence of a period of reorganization that precedes new skills.
Object permanenceInfants do have conceptual knowledge — they know objects keep existing when out of view — overturning the older "infants are merely perceptual" view.
Décalage in actionA 4-month-old is surprised when a tall object hides behind a short occluder, yet a 6-month-old may fail the same logic when objects go into containers.
We underestimate kidsWith clever designs, even infants reason about probability, logic, analogy, and causality — toddlers learn abstract "same"/"different" rules to activate a toy.

Performance is a range, not a fixed stage

Context shapes performance: prior knowledge, task complexity, and whether explicit/verbal/metacognitive skills are required. With the right support — a model to imitate, an expert's guidance — learners show high sophistication. (A fourth grader stuck on "collectivization" succeeds when a teacher covers syllables so she can read "collect," then build up.)

Neo-Piagetians argue it's incorrect to fix a learner at a stage. We all operate on any task within a range of performance — near the bottom if tired, sick, or unfamiliar; much closer to the top with appropriate support.

Takeaways from this unit

A stage theory delineates the order in which qualitatively more complex capabilities emerge, and identifies constraints on progression.

Stage theories have limits — domains shaped by instruction (literacy, math) don't fully fit, and much reasoning is already present in infancy and enriched over time.

Development continues through adolescence and adulthood — less a predictable sequence, more a result of opportunities to learn (cf. Robert Kegan's adult development).

Despite differences in values, domains, and tasks, human development is a universal process of increasing qualitative capability and complexity.

05 1.2.4 · Cultural Differences & WEIRD Science

Do these influential theories apply to all learners — or only to the ones actually studied? Critiques of early developmental research focus on the samples: which cultural contexts were studied, and who was excluded from claims about "typical" development.

WEIRD = Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, and Democratic — an acronym for the overemphasis on a small slice of the world's population. A framework can't claim to be universal unless it holds across diverse populations: cultures, ethnicities, gender identities, economic statuses, linguistic environments, disabilities, and individual traits.

The bias: inferences drawn from a limited sample, then generalized to every human being.

Case study — moral development

Prevailing late-20th-century views ranked moral development by increasing attention to fairness and justice — but that research was carried out exclusively with undergraduate men in the U.S. The 1986 study Women's Ways of Knowing (Belenky, Clinchy, Goldberger & Tarule), studying a diverse group of women, advanced a new view placing interpersonal relationships and responsibilities at the center of moral decision-making.

Alongside Carol Gilligan, these authors argued that by excluding women's voices, prior research had missed the relationally connected quality of women's knowing — tied to experience, questioning, and communities. (The critique itself has limits the authors didn't address — e.g. treating gender as a binary.)

Take a critical stance. The lesson isn't that development can't be studied scientifically — it's that developmental science must hold itself to a high standard: resist sweeping claims from limited samples, and always ask whether the available evidence supports the use you want to make of it, in your context, with your learners.

06 Glossary — quiz yourself first

Try to define each term before you reveal it.

developmental trajectory
A generalized timeline for the emergence of successively more complex skills and performances by an individual. Common in order, loose in timing — individuals conform more or less strictly to it.
social perspective-taking
What individuals understand about other people's emotions, beliefs, intentions, and likely reactions. The case-study domain used in this sequence (the Holly Dilemma).
domain of development
Areas of capability that people can develop over their life course, from infancy into adulthood. An area in which people grow in some capacity to engage with the world.
theory of mind
The ability to recognize that other people's mental states may differ from one's own. Not present at birth; obedience or moral ranking alone doesn't demonstrate it.
domain-general
Similarities in the complexity of abilities displayed across different domains. The lens behind broad stage theories — seeking shared complexity, not domain-specific detail.
stage theory
A theory that depicts development as a series of relatively discrete periods (stages) that occur in a specific order. Piaget's framework — order and constraints, but too simple to capture all of development.
décalage
The "blurriness" of stage transitions — instances where a learner's performance does not consistently fit within a stage. e.g. succeeding on object permanence behind an occluder but failing it inside a container.
WEIRD
An acronym for an overemphasis on a small slice of the world's population: Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, and Democratic. Frameworks aren't universal unless shown to hold across diverse populations.

★ Remember this

  • A trajectory is a generalized timeline — skills are common in order but variable in timing; individual differences shape rate and ceiling.
  • Theory of mind develops; it isn't innate. It's recognizing that others' mental states differ from your own — not obedience, value-ranking, or sensing danger.
  • Researchers build trajectories empirically — present a task (e.g. the Holly Dilemma) across many ages, aggregate, and compare to theorize the typical path.
  • Stage theories capture order but oversimplify. "Décalage," early infant reasoning, and context effects show performance is a range, not a fixed stage — higher with support.
  • Development runs into adulthood as opportunity-driven learning, yet remains a universal process of increasing qualitative complexity.
  • Beware WEIRD samples. Claims from Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic groups aren't universal — demand evidence across diverse populations before applying it to your learners.
