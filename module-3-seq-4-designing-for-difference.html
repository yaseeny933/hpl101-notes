Designing for Difference

Learner variability is the norm, not the exception. So design for it from the very start — don't retrofit accommodations onto a one-size lesson.

inclusion Universal Design for Learning engagement · representation · expression arts-based learning

01 The core idea

This sequence traces a single arc: from excluding learners with disabilities, to including them with accommodations, to designing learning from the start so it works for everyone. The arts turn out to be a uniquely powerful tool for that last step.

The key shift is from reactive to proactive. Old model: build one standard lesson, then bolt on accommodations for the learners it fails. New model (UDL): assume variation up front and build in multiple options so the design is "universally accessible" — necessary for some, good for all.

02 3.4.1 · Meeting the needs of all learners

For much of U.S. history, students with disabilities were excluded from general classrooms. The push for inclusion grew out of the Disability Rights Movement, in which people with disabilities advocated for their rights and self-determination.

Inclusion, and the law behind it

Inclusion = a commitment to educating every learner, as far as possible, in the school or classroom they'd attend if they had no disability. It is essentially equity-focused heterogeneous grouping that honors learning differences.

IDEA (U.S., 1990)Students must be educated in the "least restrictive environment" — starting in the same general classroom as same-age peers, with modifications and accommodations to ensure equitable opportunity to learn.
CRPD (U.N., 2006)Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities — ratified by 186 countries; recognizes the right to inclusive education at all levels, with reasonable accommodation.
Global echoesBritish Columbia (2006) and South Africa (2001) passed parallel policies — "all children and youth can learn" and need support, so systems must adapt to learners.
Henderson SchoolBoston's Dr. William W. Henderson Inclusion School (full-inclusion model) is exemplary — creating multiple pathways for students to succeed.

The limits of "inclusion" — and the better paradigm

Inclusion was monumental for its time, but advocates saw its limits: simply modifying a setting while leaving the design unchanged is not enough. (An ASL interpreter alone, for instance, may not give a deaf student equitable access to language.) The stronger, strengths-based paradigm is to assume and center learner variation and neurodiversity from the onset — taking full advantage of the strengths every learner brings.

Terminology matters. Neurodivergent is an empowering first-person descriptor of an individual; neurodiverse describes the variation that exists across a whole population or group.

03 3.4.2 · The principles of Universal Design for Learning

Universal Design for Learning (UDL) is a framework from CAST (Center for Applied Special Technology) that gives principles and guidelines for designing curricula and environments that are universally accessible to all learners, at any age and in any discipline. Its premise: learner variability is the norm, so design for everyone from the start.

The three UDL principles, each with the goal it serves. The bottom row is the ultimate aim of each column — expert, self-directed learners.

Engagement the why

Provide options that spark interest, help learners stay focused and determined on challenges, and regulate emotions and expectations.

e.g. Let students choose a book tied to their own interests; offer work-alone or work-in-pairs.

Representation the what

Provide options to perceive and comprehend information — i.e., present content in multiple formats.

e.g. Captions + transcripts on videos; reading and film versions of a novel.

Action & Expression the how

Provide options to navigate the environment and express what they know in different ways.

e.g. Demonstrate learning via essay, comic book, storyboard, or oral presentation.

Necessary for some, good for all. Nasha's captioned videos help deaf students and second-language students — and also the student watching on a noisy subway or rocking an infant to sleep. Proactive options avoid the stigma of singling people out for special help.

What's the evidence? — Learning styles are a myth

UDL is not about "learning styles." There's little evidence that people have a single fixed style (visual, auditory, etc.), and research does not support tailoring instruction to one preferred style. As Willingham argues, it's different tasks, not different people, that suit different media — every learner has a varied "toolbox of ways to think."

04 3.4.3 · UDL in practice

How do you go from wanting to design for all learners to an actual inclusive design? UDL is a process, not a checklist.

Identify the goal of the learning experience — what learners should know, do, or be if it works.

Start with the goal The first and most important step. The goal anchors everything — and separates the goal from the means of reaching it.

Brainstorm the barriers that might stop learners from reaching that goal.

Anticipate barriers Proactively, since variability is the norm. Barriers can live in the materials, the pedagogical methods, and/or the means of assessment.

Use the UDL guidelines to address those barriers with multiple options.

Provide options Offer multiple means of engagement, representation, and action & expression — flexibly chosen for this goal, not every checkpoint.

Three real examples — each highlighting one principle

Boston Arts Academy → EngagementStudents learn trigonometry through dance. (Tom Hehir's example of multiple means of engagement.)
Cell biology → RepresentationProf. Dawn Tamarkin builds physical models of cells so visually impaired students — and all students — grasp the structures.
Alternative final exam → Action & ExpressionProf. Mike Vitevitch lets students choose how to demonstrate learning — from oral presentations to origami.
Not a checklistGuidelines are flexible. Each goal needs its own options — don't try to tick every checkpoint of every principle.

Critiques & open questions

UDL isn't a cure-all. Zaloudek notes it focuses on cognitive/physical access and may miss situational and institutional barriers (family, culture, SES). Teachers may resist, or lack time, understanding, or tech skill. Evidence is stronger for improved learning processes (Capp) than for learning outcomes. And most research comes from resource-rich, "WEIRD" Global-North settings — leaving a gap for Low- and Middle-Income Countries (LMICs), despite UNESCO recommending UDL at the government level.

05 3.4.4 · The arts as uniquely powerful

The arts are a uniquely powerful tool for inclusive, accessible design — they can be woven into every part of the UDL framework.

Arts for Engagement

No single medium excites every student. Offer culturally and personally relevant arts — anime, hip-hop, music — and choice, so learners forge stronger personal connections.

e.g. OK Go Sandbox uses music videos to drive STEAM inquiry.

Arts for Representation

Use diverse mediums (drawing, sculpture, video, dance) to surface prior knowledge and embody new content; infuse visual aids and movement to support learners, especially with language-related disabilities.

e.g. "Dance Your PhD" — teaching balance & angular velocity through embodied movement.

Arts for Action & Expression

Offer assorted, uncustomary modalities for demonstrating learning — recognizing one student's favored form may not suit another.

e.g. A choreographed dance instead of a thesis; hip-hop's five elements (MCing, DJing, breaking, graffiti, knowledge) as ways to show understanding.

The arts as a distinctive way of thinking

Beyond inclusion, the arts offer something other disciplines can't. As Elliot Eisner (2002) said, "The arts celebrate multiple perspectives… there are many ways to see and interpret the world." They involve unique cognitive activities and kinds of meaning.

Motivation vs. integration

Arts as motivation (shallow)A World History teacher shows Buddhist-influenced art only to spark interest in feudal Japan — without examining the art itself or how religion shaped it.
Arts as integration (deep)Med students study a Sargent painting (El Jaleo) of a dancer through slow visual-literacy observation — then improved 38% over peers on patient-exam skills (balance, gait).

↳ Using the arts for beauty and motivation is fine — but it's only a starting point. Rigorous, integrated arts thinking fosters focus, openness to multiple interpretations, and richer inclusivity. Doing it well requires pedagogical content knowledge, which many general-ed teachers lack licensure to develop.

06 Glossary — quiz yourself first

Try to define each term before you reveal it.

inclusion
A form of heterogeneous grouping at the classroom or school level — a commitment to educating every learner, to the extent possible, in the school or classroom they would otherwise attend if they did not have special needs or a disability. Codified in the U.S. by IDEA's "least restrictive environment" requirement.
neurodivergent (vs. neurodiverse)
A first-person descriptor of an individual who is neuroatypical — often regarded as powerful and empowering. By contrast, neurodiverse describes the variation that exists across a whole population or group, not one person. "I am neurodivergent" (individual) vs. "this classroom is neurodiverse" (group).
Universal Design for Learning (UDL)
A framework designed by CAST to improve and optimize teaching and learning for all people, based on scientific insights into how humans learn. It assumes learner variability is the norm and designs for it from the start. Built on three principles: multiple means of engagement, representation, and action & expression.
multiple means of engagement
The UDL principle advising educators to give learners a variety of options to spark their interest, stay focused and determined on challenging tasks, and regulate their emotions and expectations — the "why" of learning. Offering choice of topic, or work-alone vs. work-in-pairs.
multiple means of representation
The UDL principle advising educators to give learners a variety of options to perceive and comprehend the information presented to them — the "what" of learning. Captions and transcripts on a video; a physical model of a cell alongside a microscope view.
multiple means of action and expression
The UDL principle advising educators to give learners a variety of options to navigate a learning environment and to express what they know — the "how" of learning. Letting students demonstrate learning via essay, oral presentation, storyboard, or origami.
accommodation
A modification made to the learning environment or activities so a student has equitable opportunity to learn — often added reactively to an existing design (e.g., retroactively captioning videos for one student). UDL aims to replace this retrofit with proactive, built-in options. An ASL interpreter, extra time, or added captions — necessary, but sometimes not sufficient.
CAST
The Center for Applied Special Technology — the research organization that articulated and maintains the UDL framework and guidelines. Author of the UDL Guidelines (version 2.2, 2018).
learning styles (myth)
The popular but unsupported idea that each person learns best in one fixed "style" (visual, auditory, etc.). Research finds little evidence for fixed styles, and does not support tailoring instruction to a single preferred style — it's tasks, not people, that suit particular media. Willingham: every learner has a varied "toolbox of ways to think."

07 Practice & discussion

Answer first, then reveal. A mix of recall and applying UDL to scenarios.

recallWhat is the single most important first step in a UDL design process?
Identify the goal of the learning experience — what learners will know, do, or be if it's effective. Only after the goal is clear do you brainstorm barriers and then provide options. (Separating the goal from the means is what lets you offer flexible paths to it.)
applyA professor adds high-quality captions and downloadable transcripts to every assigned video at the start of the semester. Which UDL principle is this, and why is it "good for all"?
This is multiple means of representation — giving options to perceive/comprehend the content. It's "necessary for some" (deaf, hard-of-hearing, second-language students) but good for all: anyone watching on a noisy subway or while rocking an infant benefits, and building it in proactively avoids stigmatizing those who'd otherwise have to request an accommodation.
applyA psychology professor lets students choose how to show their learning on the final — oral presentation, written paper, or even origami. Classify this UDL move.
Multiple means of action and expression — options for how learners express what they know. (This is the Mike Vitevitch "alternative final exam" example from 3.4.3.)
applyStudents at Boston Arts Academy learn trigonometry through dance. Which UDL principle does this most illustrate — and is this the only principle it could touch?
It most illustrates multiple means of engagement (sparking interest/motivation in the math). But note: a single rich activity often illustrates several principles at once — dance could also support representation (embodying the concept) and expression. UDL guidelines are flexible, not a checklist.
recallName two well-documented critiques or limits of UDL discussed in this sequence.
Any two of: (1) it centers cognitive/physical access and may miss situational/institutional barriers (family, culture, SES) — Zaloudek; (2) teachers may resist it or lack time, understanding, or tech skill; (3) stronger evidence for improved learning processes than outcomes; (4) most research is from WEIRD/high-income settings, leaving a gap for LMICs.

Open-ended discussion prompts

  1. Inclusion was a "monumental shift," yet advocates pushed past it. In your own context, where do you see learning still being retrofitted with accommodations rather than designed for difference from the start?
  2. The sequence argues "learning styles" are a myth while championing multiple means of representation. How would you explain the difference to a colleague who thinks these are the same idea?
  3. When do the arts merely motivate a subject versus being deeply integrated into it (as in the El Jaleo / med-school example)? What would deep arts integration look like in a subject you teach or care about?

★ Remember this

  • Variability is the norm. Design for the full range of learners from the start — don't retrofit accommodations onto a one-size lesson.
  • From exclusion → inclusion → universal design. Inclusion (least restrictive environment) was a milestone; UDL is the stronger, proactive, strengths-based paradigm.
  • Three UDL principles: multiple means of engagement (the why), representation (the what), and action & expression (the how).
  • Necessary for some, good for all. Built-in options remove barriers and avoid the stigma of singling learners out.
  • Process, not checklist. Set the goal → anticipate barriers → provide flexible options. Learning styles are a myth.
  • The arts are uniquely powerful — woven across all three principles, and most potent when deeply integrated, not just used to motivate.
