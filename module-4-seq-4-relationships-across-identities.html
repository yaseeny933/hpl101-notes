Engaging Across Identities

Diversity alone doesn't teach. Learning across difference takes care, social-emotional skill, bridging ties, and partnership with families.

climate of care social-emotional learning bonding · bridging family engagement

01 The core idea

Module 3 argued that diversity can spark contact with ideas and identities unlike our own. But putting different people in a room does not guarantee learning. Without equity, inclusion, and trust, diverse groups slide into conflict, silence, and harm.

The throughline of this sequence: learners can only take the risk of sharing ideas and engaging across difference when they feel safe, seen, and that they belong. Building that climate is relational work — it runs through care, social-emotional learning (SEL), the deliberate building of bridging ties across groups, and genuine partnership with families and communities.

02 4.4.1 — Building Relationships through Care

Positive peer relationships depend on school / organizational climate — the felt sense that everyone belongs. Tackling something like bullying is therefore systemic work across policy, practice, and culture, which is harder (and more effective) than disciplining individual students.

Interpersonal violence is a need-meeting strategy

Bullying tends to erupt where youth struggle to manage emotions or take others' perspectives, or where there are unbalanced power dynamics or unmet needs. Harmful behavior is often a child trying to meet their own needs at others' expense. It can be a barrier to learning for both the bully and the bullied — who may be the same person in different contexts.

Develops with ageYounger children (less perspective-taking) use direct words or physical aggression; older children use social aggression — damaging another's status or relationships.
Often identity-targetedBullying frequently targets identity — e.g. LGBTQIA+ youth — raising the sequence's driving question: how do we bring learners into productive engagement across difference?
24/7 reachStudents describe survival mode: "just getting through the day." Social media means harassment no longer "stops at 3:30" (Maxwell, 2016).
Culture, not policingSchools "can't police or surveil their way to cultures of care." Overly punitive measures undermine trusting, restorative environments.
The constructive answer to a harmful climate is social-emotional learning (SEL): a fundamental part of human development through which people acquire the social, emotional, behavioral, and character skills needed to live, work, learn, and make decisions with others.

How SEL skills develop

Per Stephanie Jones and HGSE's EASEL Lab (Ecological Approaches to SEL), SEL is both an independent skill set and a subset of academic skills — so it lives in "teachable moments" across the whole school day, not just dedicated lessons. SEL Kernels are small, embeddable tools and activities educators can drop into varied contexts. But SEL must be deliberately built in; it doesn't happen by accident. The movement against SEL

Despite strong evidence, SEL has been politicized and caricatured in the U.S. Critics claim it's caregivers' job alone, that components like empathy clash with families' beliefs, or that SEL is a cover for teaching critical race theory. The course treats these claims as unfounded and dangerous: denying students SEL can harm growth and learning, socially and academically. (Caregivers do play a vital role — but that doesn't displace SEL in schools.)

04 4.4.2 — SEL in Practice

There's no single "right" way to do SEL. It can run at three scales — the learner, the program, and the system level — and be taught as its own domain or woven into other subjects.

At the learner level — Simple Interactions

The Simple Interactions framework (from earlier in Module 4) turns ordinary moments into SEL. Its four threads — connection, reciprocity, inclusion, and opportunities to grow — let adults build developmental relationships in everyday life.

Concrete case: after a dull one-on-one reading assessment, a teacher pulls a struggling student aside to name the incremental progress they've made — even though they're not yet at state-standard proficiency. The student connects effort, not innate talent, to their growth — a mindset shift that builds self-efficacy and motivation. "No human interaction is ever just about one single thing."

At the program / system level

SEL integrated with other domains embedded

SEL can be part of academic learning itself. Vicki Zakrzewski (Greater Good Science Center) notes the Common Core math standard asks students to "justify conclusions, communicate them, and respond to others' arguments" — which requires listening, perspective-taking, and reading others' feelings.

payoff Prosocial behavior in adolescence (cooperating, sharing, helping) links to better mental health, self-esteem, and peer relationships in adulthood (Do, Guassi Moreira, & Telzer, 2017).

SEL as a domain in itself explicit

SEL can be taught directly — strategies for attention, self-regulation of emotion and impulse, and social perspective-taking.

case Summit Preparatory Charter High (Redwood City, CA) runs a weekly course — "Habits, Community, and Culture" — whose activities each map onto an EASEL SEL skill category (Minero, 2017).

Six guidelines for embedding SEL in a culture (Jones / EASEL Lab)

SEL isn't only curriculum — many schools and workplaces infuse it into practices, policies, and culture (building the climates of care from 4.4.1). To do it well, organizations should:

Allot enough time.

integrate Make SEL part of the curriculum and day-to-day practice — not an "extra" done with leftover time.

Extend SEL beyond the classroom.

everywhere Include the lunchroom, playground, athletic fields — especially spaces where learners feel least safe.

Apply skills in real time.

in the moment Coach learners during day-to-day interactions, seizing "teachable moments."

Ensure staff support and training.

adults first Adults need their own social-emotional awareness to help students — ongoing training is key.

Facilitate ownership and buy-in.

stakeholders Align programs with community needs and design them with participants.

Use data to inform decisions.

accountability Even simple tools — climate surveys, behavior-referral analysis — let schools monitor and hold themselves accountable.

SEL for a changing future — and 21st-century skills

21st-century skills ("soft skills") are a broad, domain-general set deemed essential for today's world. Economists Murnane & Levy (who coined the term) frame them as the human strengths — ingenuity, creativity, social interaction — that computers can't replicate. Broader frameworks (e.g. Partnership for 21st-Century Learning) extend them to thriving in life and citizenship.

The "4 Cs"One popular distillation: critical thinking, communication, collaboration, creativity (Common Sense Media).
Overlap with SELNot identical (e.g. media literacy is a 21st-century skill but not SEL), yet collaboration and communication are core to both.
The development gapDeming (2017): social skills are learned early, de-emphasized in K–12 for academics, then re-emphasized in the workforce.
Close the gapGive K–12 students abundant SEL practice — collaborative learning, group projects — to pay dividends in a changing future.

05 4.4.3 — Bonding Skills & Bridging Skills

Step back to a bigger frame: social capital. "Capital" means a set of assets. Financial capital is tangible (money); human capital is intangible — the skills, capabilities, and health of people (when districts plan "human capital development," they mean hiring and growing great educators). Social capital is different again: the assets in a person's social networks, plus the trust and cooperation among the people in them (Putnam and others).

Bonding social capital strong ties

Relationships with our in-group — people who are like us. These networks are denser and form easily by affinity.

e.g. family · close friends · members of one's own identity group(s).

Bridging social capital weak ties

Relationships outside our in-group — ties to other groups. They take more effort to nurture, "bridging" from one bonded network to another.

e.g. friends of friends · members of other identity group(s) · acquaintances who help you find a job.

↳ Danielle Allen (HGSE / Safra Center) argues that building bridging capital across identity groups is critical for colleges — yet many institutions don't know how to support it. Chambliss & Takacs likewise show that broader weak-tie networks strengthen students' sense of belonging in college.

Tatum's ABC Approach

Beverly Daniel Tatum (former Spelman College president) offers three things institutions must do together, each building on the last, to support engagement across identities — especially for learners with historically excluded identities:

A — Affirming Identity

see yourself Everyone should see themselves reflected — in curriculum, in faculty/staff/leadership, and in available spaces and groups.

B — Building Community

engage across Help students engage across identities through events, curriculum, and modeled dialogue toward a common purpose.

C — Cultivating Leadership

boundary-crossing Prepare students to lead in a pluralistic society — skills of working within and across identity groups, leading discussions of race and identity.

06 4.4.4 — Family & Community Engagement

Can learners show up as their full selves if their families and communities aren't also embraced? The school door can be a "closed door more than an open one." Improving outcomes in culturally responsive ways requires collaboration with caregivers and communities, not educators working alone. The key question: in what ways, how often, and with what supports can educators include families in decision-making and in everyday teaching and learning?

Karen Mapp's work (HGSE) — the "8 for 8" and the dual capacity-building framework (Mapp & Kuttner, 2013) — frames the goal as building two capacities at once: building up schools to serve families and building up families to engage effectively with schools. The framework specifies the opportunity conditions required for effective family–school partnerships.

Case study — Stanton Elementary (Washington, DC)

In 2010 the struggling charter school was put under new leadership to "turn around." A parent: "These were elementary school kids, and they were running the school. Parents were disconnected, staff and families were battling one another…" The principal's first moves — better instruction, behavior management, school culture — weren't enough.

1 · Teacher home visitsIn 2011 teachers began visiting students' homes — meeting families on their own ground.
2 · Caregiver–teacher teams"Teams" of teachers and caregivers met to work collaboratively to support students.

↳ By the end of the 2012 school year, performance began improving. The course frames Stanton as evidence that the opportunity conditions in Mapp & Kuttner's framework were met by the family-engagement initiative — a real-world test of dual capacity-building.

07 Glossary — quiz yourself first

Try to define each term before you reveal it.

social-emotional learning (SEL)
The social, emotional, behavioral, and character skills necessary to live, work, learn, and make decisions with others — including cognitive regulation, emotional processes, social / interpersonal skills, character, and mindset. It's both an independent skill set and a subset of academic skills; it lives in everyday "teachable moments."
climate of care
A school or organizational culture that fosters belonging, trust, and positive relationships among all learners — built through systemic, restorative practice rather than punitive policing or surveillance. The constructive alternative to bullying cultures; SEL is its engine.
social aggression
Harm that damages another person's social status or relationships (e.g. exclusion, rumor-spreading) — more common among older children with more developed perspective-taking, versus the direct/physical aggression of younger children.
21st-century skills
A broad set of knowledge, skills, work habits, and character traits believed to be critically important to success in today's world. The "4 Cs" — critical thinking, communication, collaboration, creativity. Overlaps with, but isn't identical to, SEL.
Simple Interactions
A developmental framework for weaving SEL into everyday moments through four threads: connection, reciprocity, inclusion, and opportunities to grow — used to build developmental relationships across ages and contexts.
social capital
Assets composed of a person's social networks and the trust and cooperation of the people in those networks. Contrast with human capital (people's skills/health) and financial capital (tangible money).
bonding social capital
Relationships with those in our "in-group" — people who are like us in some meaningful way. Denser networks formed easily by affinity ("strong ties"). Family, close friends, members of one's own identity group(s).
bridging social capital
Relationships with those outside our in-group — ties to other groups. They take more effort to nurture, "bridging" from one bonded network to another ("weak ties"). Friends of friends, members of other identity groups, an acquaintance who helps you find a job.
ABC Approach (Tatum)
Three interdependent practices for engagement across identities: Affirming Identity, Building Community, and Cultivating Leadership — each building on the previous.
dual capacity-building framework
Mapp & Kuttner's (2013) framework for family–school partnerships that builds two capacities at once: building up schools to serve families and families to engage effectively with schools, by establishing the necessary "opportunity conditions."
family & community engagement
Partnering with caregivers and communities — in decision-making and in everyday teaching and learning — so that fostering belonging, achievement, and social-emotional growth succeeds. (See Mapp's "8 for 8"; the Stanton Elementary case.)

08 Practice & Discussion

Answer each before revealing. Mix of recall and application.

recallWhy does the course say diversity alone is not enough for learning?
Because simply placing different people together doesn't ensure learning. Without equity, inclusion, and trust, diverse groups can produce conflict, silence, and harm. Learners need to feel safe and that they belong before they'll take the risk of sharing ideas or being open to others' views.
recallName the four threads of the Simple Interactions framework.
Connection, reciprocity, inclusion, and opportunities to grow. Together they let adults build developmental relationships and weave SEL into everyday moments.
applyA new student is regularly pressured to hand over their lunch but won't report it, fearing reprisal. Through this sequence's lens, how should a school respond?
Treat it as a systemic climate issue, not just one student's misbehavior. Avoid purely punitive/surveillance responses, which undermine trust. Look for the unmet needs and power dynamics driving the behavior, build a climate of care through SEL (perspective-taking, emotion regulation, belonging), and use restorative rather than punitive approaches. Recognize bullying harms both the bullied and the bully.
applyA first-gen college student knows classmates but no one in their target industry. Which type of social capital is short, and what would Tatum's ABC approach do about it?
They have bonding capital (in-group strong ties) but lack bridging capital (weak ties to other groups, e.g. the "acquaintance who helps you find a job"). Tatum's approach: Affirm the student's identity so they see themselves reflected, Build community through deliberate dialogue across identities, and Cultivate leadership / boundary-crossing skills — institutions must actively support bridging rather than assume it happens on its own.
applyA principal improves instruction and discipline but scores stay flat. Using Stanton Elementary, what's the likely missing ingredient?
Family and community engagement. At Stanton, instruction/behavior/culture changes alone didn't suffice; performance improved only after adding teacher home visits and caregiver–teacher teams. The principle is dual capacity-building (Mapp & Kuttner): build schools' capacity to serve families and families' capacity to engage — meeting the "opportunity conditions."

★ Remember this

  • Diversity needs care to become learning. Safety, trust, and belonging are preconditions for engaging across difference.
  • Bullying is systemic. It signals unmet needs and power imbalances; you can't police your way to a climate of care — SEL and restorative practice can build one.
  • SEL is a developmental skill set, embedded in everyday moments (Simple Interactions, SEL Kernels) and across three scales — learner, program, system. Six EASEL guidelines keep it from being an "extra."
  • Bonding vs. bridging. In-group strong ties form easily; bridging weak ties across identities take deliberate effort — and Tatum's ABC (Affirm, Build, Cultivate) shows institutions how.
  • Families are partners, not visitors. Dual capacity-building (Mapp & Kuttner) and Stanton Elementary show learning improves when schools and families build each other up.
  • SEL also future-proofs learners, overlapping with 21st-century "4 Cs" skills computers can't replace.
