Variation in Developing Learners

Learners differ in where they start and how fast they grow. Variation isn't the exception — in most domains it's exactly what we should expect.

starting points rate of growth neurodiversity mindset & support

01 The core idea

To understand learning we have to notice when it begins, how it progresses, and how both vary across learners. Developmental theories predict when capabilities tend to emerge — but they don't tell us much about any one individual.

Developing learners naturally vary in their starting points and their rates of growth, both within and across domains. This variation is driven by a mix of biology, experience, and the level & quality of task support. The educator's job is to understand these sources of variation so every learner gets an enriching environment — not to design for an "average" that describes no one.

Meet Seth & Dennis (the running scenario)

In first grade, Seth has a vocabulary of ~5,000 words and reads chapter books; Dennis knows ~1,000 words and reads picture books. Same grade, very different starting points. We return to them throughout the sequence to ask: does this gap justify different reading groups? Will they ever converge? What support would help?

02 3.1.1 · Starting Points

A starting point is the time at which a learner first acquires some new knowledge or skill. Even for universal skills like language, starting points vary widely: one study of 2-year-olds estimated vocabularies ranging from 50 to 550 words.

Experience-expectant vs. experience-dependent

Experience-expectant expected

Skills that emerge early because of experiences nearly all humans share — seeing faces, hearing voices, interacting with caregivers. The brain is "set up from the start" to respond to these reliable inputs during sensitive periods.

e.g. Acquiring a native spoken language; vision; forming attachment. Intervention = ensure access to ordinary experiences (e.g., screen infants for vision/hearing).

Experience-dependent depends

Skills whose emergence depends on the learner accessing specific experiences. Tied to lifelong neural plasticity. Most of formal education targets this kind of learning.

e.g. Reading print, algebra word problems, test-anxiety regulation. Intervention = explicit strategies, scaffolding, and access to materials (e.g., books).

↳ Why distinguish them? To focus intervention. A young brain expects to hear language; it does not expect to decode arbitrary symbols on paper — that depends on instruction. (Note: blind children may show delayed language but ultimately master it equivalent to sighted peers — Pérez-Pereira, 2006.)

Words produced by percentile, 16–30 months (after Fenson et al., 1994). Lines start close, then fan apart.

The jaggedness principleAny individual's profile is "jagged" — above the mean on some tasks, below on others that seem similar. We can't use one-dimensional thinking for abilities built from many weakly-related dimensions (Todd Rose, The End of Average).
Design for the edges, not the averageFaced with varied incoming writers, the best move isn't matching the "average" or adding remedial tracks (deficit identity) — it's redesigning for a wider range of engagement and assessment.

03 3.1.2 · Rate of Growth

Rate of growth = how fast a developing learner grows within a particular domain. There is enormous variation here, which raises a warning: trends and averages can be misinterpreted to define "normal," masking the natural range of human variation.

Matthew Effect rich get richer

Early advantage leads to later success: those who start ahead both benefit more from instruction and get more access to challenging instruction. Small starting-point gaps compound into large ones.

e.g. Seth reads more → wider vocabulary → harder books → more knowledge → advanced classes. Dennis decides reading "isn't fun," reads only when required, and falls further behind. (Based on Stanovich, 2009: vocabulary ↔ reading ↔ frequency of reading all reinforce each other.)

Catch-up Effect converge

Regardless of starting point, learners reach the same level — they "catch up." Typical of experience-expectant skills that emerge without instruction.

e.g. Walking. Two 9-month-olds may look very different (one needs help to stand, one walks with assistance), but by 15 months both walk unaided; by age 5 ambulatory children walk about equally well. The later starter simply grew faster.

Same starting-point gap, two fates — depending on whether the domain is experience-dependent (reading) or experience-expectant (walking).

↳ Reading is experience-dependent, so instructional decisions (how we group learners, how responsive we are) can exacerbate initial differences. Personalization — support tailored to an individual to mitigate starting-point differences and/or boost rate of growth — is the lever. Watch out: expectations affect supports. One study found parents talk more to 13-month-olds who walk than those who crawl (Paterson et al., 2016) — a delay in one domain can shrink the input in another.

04 3.1.3 · Is There Such a Thing as Atypical Development?

Atypical development = developing differently from what is expected of others the same age — at either end of the range. Developmental trajectories tell us when capabilities tend to emerge for most; they say little about any individual.

The Polgár sisters — atypical at the high end

In the 1970s László Polgár began intensive, early chess instruction with his three daughters (teaching them himself, hiring expert tutors, and insisting they compete against men). The result: Susan became a Grandmaster, Sofia an International Master, and Judit a Grandmaster at age 15 — younger than Bobby Fischer. A demonstration of how rich, early, focused instruction can produce atypical development (Feldman, 1993).

↳ This is also a Matthew Effect: high early skill earned them games against stronger players, accelerating their growth — just as Seth's early vocabulary earned him harder books.

Neurodiversity is the term for the natural variation we observe in human development and learning. Crucially, it includes those with developmental and learning disabilities and those deemed prodigies — and it means never losing sight of any learner's strengths alongside their limitations.
DyslexiaA specific disorder of acquiring reading skills, linked to reduced grey matter in left-hemisphere regions — differences detectable before school starts. Early identification opens the door to early intervention (Raschle, Chang & Gaab, 2011).
Autism spectrum (ASD)Associated with limited/delayed theory of mind (social perspective-taking). Structured, integrated play groups — scaffolding children with and without autism to play together — increase social play (Bass & Mulick, 2007; Wolfberg et al., 2015).

Two cautions the unit insists on: (1) Access to early intervention is not equally distributed — it's time- and resource-intensive, and inequities in race/ethnicity, income, and language proficiency make it harder to reach. (2) Diagnostic categories are often necessary to secure support, but there's a risk of treating learners as if they are their disabilities. Remembering that all learners have jagged profiles guards against essentializing a disability and lowering expectations unjustifiably.

05 3.1.4 · Task Support & Mindset

So far, variation has felt largely given. This unit covers the two levers we can pull: the support around the task, and the mindset inside the learner.

How task support affects growth

Appropriate support extends the range of what a learner can do — working in the learner's Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD).

ZPD With high support a learner reaches a high level of performance, which becomes the starting point for the next skill. With low support, the same learner performs lower.

For atypical learners especially, support must be calibrated — too low overwhelms, too high oversimplifies.

Calibration Learning improves when educators understand a learner's current abilities and strike the balance. Integrated play groups give autistic youth just enough social scaffolding.

It's not just how much support, but its quality and type.

Quality > quantity Math-anxious parents/teachers can transmit anxiety; more parental math input can sometimes lower achievement. Quantity vs. quality recurs everywhere (words heard vs. quality of interaction; drill vs. deliberate practice).

↳ Timing matters: high-quality pre-K (coached teachers, evidence-based curricula) raises proficiency in literacy, math, executive function, and emotion recognition (Weiland et al., 2013), with long-term academic benefits (McCoy et al., 2017) — partly by increasing children's ability to benefit from later schooling.

Growth mindset vs. fixed mindset (Carol Dweck)

Growth mindset malleable

The belief that level of intelligence can be altered by effort and experience. Associated with better persistence through academic challenges and faster improvement.

Fueled by praise for effort: "Great job working hard on this!" Encourages spending more time on the task.

Fixed mindset innate

The belief that intelligence is innate and cannot be changed. Can discourage practice ("Why bother? I'll never be good at this"), slowing improvement.

Fueled by praise for ability: "You're so smart / gifted at science!" Aparna learns to see her intelligence as fixed.

↳ Mindset is not a fixed trait — most of us blend both, varying by domain and moment. The useful question isn't "are you growth or fixed?" but "when, where, and with whom do I act as if I am — and why?" And per Dweck (2015), don't just praise effort and stop: tell the truth about a learner's achievement, then support their progress.

06 Glossary — quiz yourself first

Try to define each term before you reveal it.

starting point
The time at which a learner first acquires some new knowledge or skill. Seth and Dennis enter first grade at very different vocabulary starting points (~5,000 vs. ~1,000 words).
experience-expectant
The descriptor for skills that emerge early in life as a result of common experiences nearly all humans have — e.g., seeing others communicate, interacting with communicative adults, forming attachment with reliable caregivers. Acquiring a native spoken language; vision.
experience-dependent
The descriptor for skills whose emergence and elaboration depend on the nature of a learner's experience — e.g., later language and literacy skills and some social-emotional skills (regulating test anxiety, sustaining belief in the value of effort despite obstacles). Reading printed material; solving algebra word problems.
jaggedness principle
The principle that we cannot apply simplified, one-dimensional thinking to characterize something that consists of multiple dimensions only weakly related to one another. A student strong at spatial geometry but weak at working memory — math ability isn't a single number.
rate of growth
The rate at which a developing learner grows within a particular domain. A late-walking 9-month-old can grow faster to catch up by 15 months.
Matthew Effect
A phenomenon in which early advantage or success leads to later success in learning — "the rich get richer." Those who start ahead benefit more from instruction and gain more access to challenging instruction. Seth's early reading skill earns him harder books and faster growth; Dennis falls further behind.
catch-up effect
A phenomenon in which learners attain similar levels of performance in a domain despite early differences in starting points in that domain. Walking — by around age 5 ambulatory children walk about equally well regardless of when they started.
personalization
Support tailored to an individual's needs, intended to mitigate differences in starting points and/or promote a person's rate of growth. Giving the two 9-month-olds different kinds of help so both reach independent walking.
neurodiversity
A term describing the natural variation we observe in human development and learning — including those with developmental and learning disabilities and those deemed prodigies — emphasizing strengths as well as limitations. Dyslexia, ADHD, and autism alongside chess prodigies all fall within natural variation.
atypical development
Developing differently from what is expected of other people the same age — at either the high or low end of the typical range. The Polgár sisters' chess prodigy development (Feldman, 1993).
growth mindset
The belief that level of intelligence is altered based on effort and experience (Carol Dweck). Fostered by praising effort: "You worked hard on this."
fixed mindset
The belief that intelligence is innate and cannot be changed (Carol Dweck). Reinforced by praising ability: "You're so smart / gifted."

07 Practice & discussion

Answer in your head first, then reveal. Mix of recall and application.

Q1Which is an example of an experience-expectant skill: eating with a utensil, verbal communication in a native language, or playing a sport?
Verbal communication in a native language. It emerges early because of experiences nearly all humans share. Eating with a utensil and playing a sport are experience-dependent — they emerge based on the particular nature of a learner's experiences.
Q2A learner loves spatial tasks but struggles with working memory: she aces geometry but forgets to write down homework and earns a B-. Which principle does this illustrate?
The jaggedness principle. Math performance is built from multiple, only weakly-related dimensions, so a single grade flattens a jagged profile. (Compare: a one-mile run time or a vocabulary-comprehension score is one-dimensional and would not illustrate jaggedness.)
Q3Mrs. Bingham lets students choose, after a shortened math block, to keep doing math or read silently. What's the most likely outcome?
The math-choosing group steadily gains math skills and ends up outperforming the readers in math — a Matthew Effect inside one classroom. Students who already like (and are better at) math choose more math and pull further ahead, while math-avoiders learn less.
Q4Incoming first-years show widely varied writing ability. Which response best leverages the jaggedness principle: match the curriculum to the "average," add remedial seminars, or redesign for a wider range of engagement and assessment?
Redesign the seminars to offer more options for engagement and a wider range of means for performance assessment. Designing for the "average" leaves some unsupported and others unchallenged; remedial tracks miss the full range and risk a deficit-oriented identity.
Q5A professor tells Gerardo: "I can tell you worked hard on a challenging topic; there's a lot to improve, but keep putting in effort and you'll gain deep understanding." Which reaction does growth-mindset theory predict?
He starts thinking about what he can do to keep making progress. The feedback praises effort and tackling a challenge (not innate "intelligence") and tells the truth about where to improve — exactly the combination Dweck says builds a growth mindset.

Open-ended discussion prompts

  1. Does the initial vocabulary gap justify placing Seth and Dennis in different reading groups? If you grouped them, what would you do to keep the Matthew Effect from widening the gap?
  2. How can educators' words and actions support or constrain a learner's atypical development — at either end of the range — without essentializing a diagnosis?
  3. "Quality over quantity" of support recurs across domains. Pick a domain you teach or know well: what would high-quality (not just high-quantity) support actually look like in practice?

★ Remember this

  • Variation is the norm. In most domains learners differ widely in starting points and rates of growth — expect it, design for it.
  • Two flavors of starting point. Experience-expectant skills need ordinary shared experiences; experience-dependent skills need specific access and instruction.
  • Matthew vs. Catch-up. In experience-dependent domains early gaps compound (rich get richer); in experience-expectant ones learners converge. Personalization can bend the curve.
  • Profiles are jagged, and atypical cuts both ways. Neurodiversity includes disabilities and prodigies alike — never lose sight of strengths or lower expectations unjustifiably.
  • Support is a lever — and so is mindset. Calibrate support to the ZPD (quality > quantity); praise effort and tell the truth to grow a growth mindset.
  • Don't design for the average. The "average" learner describes no one — equity means giving every learner an enriching environment.
