HPL101 · Module 3 · Sequence 2

Culture, Environment & Resources

Learner variability isn't just inside the head. Culture, money, and the way schools define "smart" shape who gets supported — and who gets labeled lacking.

culture as a "web of meaning" socioeconomic status social construction of smartness assets, not deficits

01 The core idea

Sequence 1 looked at how learning unfolds inside the individual. This sequence zooms out to the contexts that surround learners — and how those contexts are rarely neutral.

Three forces — culture, socioeconomic resources, and the social construction of "smartness" — shape learners' starting points, the supports they receive, and the labels attached to them. The danger is the deficit lens: reading difference as deficiency. The corrective is an asset lens that recognizes every learner's funds of knowledge and asks schools to adapt to children rather than the reverse.

The central move of this sequence: shift from deficit framing to an asset / funds-of-knowledge framing.

02 What's culture got to do with learning? 3.2.1

Culture is the lens through which a group makes sense of the world — anthropologist Clifford Geertz called it the "web of meaning." Much of it is invisible; you usually only notice it when it's violated or when you must adapt to an unfamiliar set of expectations.

Zaretta Hammond's "Culture Tree" — three levels

Surface Observable, concrete elementsFood, dress, music, holidays — the visible "leaves."
Shallow Unspoken rules & norms of everyday interactionPersonal space, eye contact, nonverbal communication — how we enact deep-culture values.
Deep Tacit knowledge & unconscious worldview ("the roots")Assumptions that guide ethics and the mental models we use to process new information — including beliefs about who is responsible for teaching and learning.
Deep culture shapes who "owns" learning. Many U.S. caregivers believe they must be a child's first teacher, preparing the child to succeed independently in school. Many Mexican caregivers, by contrast, see family and school as part of a larger community that collaborates to educate children — their role is to ensure the child is loved and well-behaved, while "school skills" belong to teachers (Bush & Peterson, 2013; Espinosa, 1995). Neither is right or wrong; they're different priorities.

Emeritus HGSE professor Robert LeVine: all families, across cultures, want similar long-term outcomes — health, happiness, educational success, economic viability, warm family ties. They differ in how they prioritize these (e.g., family ties vs. achievement; autonomy vs. obedience; curiosity vs. tradition).

When the mismatch turns harmful: when educators' deep-culture beliefs clash with those of learners and families, it can detract from — and even harm — learning. A classroom unwittingly designed to favor children who already know the alphabet and can count to 100 disadvantages a child who instead learned to help run a household and care for younger siblings. Teachers may then form negative judgments about those learners and their families. Documented among Mexican-American families and U.S. schools, this mismatch can fuel damaging stereotypes (Valencia & Black, 2002). Caveat: there is enormous variability within any group — research patterns do not apply to every family.

03 Socioeconomic differences 3.2.2

Core question: How do the resources available to a child — financial, interpersonal, social — affect their ability to learn? In the U.S., access to money strongly shapes access to educationally relevant experiences, and income gaps are high and increasing.

Sean Reardon — the income achievement gap

HGSE grad / Stanford professor. Historical analysis of family incomes and child-focused spending since the 1950s shows a widening gap in resources between rich and poor families — with educational consequences.

Resources → opportunities to learn

Museum visits, theater and arts, travel, tutoring, coaching, high-quality preschool. At the same age, children with more resources often show higher performance — an echo of the Matthew Effect ("the rich get richer").

The "performance vs. access to resources" graph (all children age 5) reframes the usual age-on-the-x-axis chart: among same-age children, greater resources tend to track with greater task performance — e.g., larger early vocabularies for children in high-quality preschool.

Read the research critically. The "30 million word gap" — a hypothesized gap in language exposure between children in highly-educated, financially-stable, two-parent homes versus single-parent, financially-constrained, lower-education homes — is one of the most debated findings in social science. Critics (e.g., Kamenetz, 2018) argue it embeds cultural assumptions about which kinds of talk "count." The course presents it as a case for examining hidden cultural assumptions in educational research, not as settled fact.

04 The social construction of "smartness" 3.2.3

"Smartness" is socially situated and socially constructed. What counts as smart depends on what's valued (shallow-culture practices), and what's valued is implicitly tied to the dominant deep culture of schools and society.

Seth & Dennis, revisited

Seth — placed in an advanced reading group in first grade — keeps being exposed to rich texts and is happily called on to read. Dennis disengages early, struggles when asked to read aloud. By third grade these interactions entrench who gets labeled "smart" in the minds of both boys, their classmates, and the teacher. A small early sorting compounds into divergent trajectories.

Case study — Beth Rubin (2007): constructing competence in high school

A ninth-grade social studies classroom in a large public high school serving mostly low-income students of color. Researchers documented norms that signaled what kind of learning was expected:

Mr. Bartlett: "Does anyone know the difference between communism and capitalism?" … then abruptly cuts off a rich discussion of government to administer a planned test.

Kareem: "Why they got to say 'European luxury?' Why not 'Black luxury?'" — a sharp, content-related question. Instead of using it as a point of connection, the teacher removes Kareem from the room "for creating a disturbance."

Substitute: "Look like you are doing something and I won't bother you." Students are literally told to pretend to learn.

Rubin's point: a steady diet of low-level tasks, demands for compliance, and humiliation signals to students what kind of learning is possible and expected of them. A review by the Education Commission of the States found teachers' negative expectations account for ~5–10% of the variance in student achievement (Workman, 2012).

Where the bias hides — it's not always obvious

Too little

Teachers who underestimate students overlook their rich contributions to class.

Too much (also a problem)

Over-scaffolding only some students caps how far they can stretch — limiting work at the top of their ZPD. Empty praise ("good effort!") without truthful, targeted feedback doesn't promote a growth mindset.

The legacy of intelligence testing

For much of the 19th–20th centuries, many psychologists believed in a fixed, innate "general intelligence" (g), measured as an IQ (e.g., the Stanford-Binet, devised by Lewis Terman). Some test designers believed intelligence varied by race and ethnicity — a deep-culture belief baked into the tests, which then "confirmed" itself when Northern/Western European Protestant test-takers scored highest. These results were cited to support eugenics and immigration restriction. Stephen Jay Gould (1982) showed the bias concretely: the Army Beta asked recent immigrants to spot missing tennis courts, phonographs, and light bulbs. Historian Ibram X. Kendi argues the very notion of an "achievement gap" was built into the design of these tests and reverberates today.

05 Moving beyond deviance, risk & deficit 3.2.4

Documenting group differences (Reardon, the word gap) risks an unwanted reading: that some groups are "deficient." This unit challenges that interpretation directly.

Education professor Luis Moll reframes the discourse with "funds of knowledge" — "the historically accumulated and culturally developed bodies of knowledge and skills essential for household or individual functioning and well-being" (Moll, Amanti, Neff & Gonzalez, 1992). Every learner brings these from home and community. The focus shifts from deficiencies → assets.

Difference is not deficiency

Learners vary by ethnicity, culture, class, and experience — and most of these differences carry no inherent disadvantage for learning. Saying "flapjack" instead of "pancake," or using regional grammar, only becomes a "problem" when framed as a departure from a dominant norm. Correcting the child who says "flapjack" can undermine their confidence and identity as a competent speaker.

Relocate the "risk"

As in Module 1: labeling a learner "at-risk" implies the risk lives inside the child — rather than emerging from a confrontation between the child and a setting built for others' needs. Enforce a single normative standard and some learners inevitably fall short (don't speak standard English, lack story-book exposure, don't fit gendered expectations).

If we shift to a mindset in which schools are expected to adapt to children, then learners' funds of knowledge become a resource and a foundation for further learning. — Unit 3.2.4

Higher ed is shifting too: students from nondominant identities bring "cultural wealth" as resources for learning and resilience (Yosso, 2005; Longmire-Avital, 2019). For many educators, seeing what learners know as assets rather than deficits requires a genuine conceptual change — disrupting the prior view that some learners are simply "lacking." (Carla Shalaby's Troublemakers reframes "problem" children as signs that school cultures over-constrain what children can be.)

06 Glossary — quiz yourself first

Try to define each term before you reveal it.

culture
The "web of meaning" (Geertz) — the multidimensional array of rules, beliefs, relationships, and commitments that guide our behavior and infuse our lives with significance. Often invisible until it's violated or you must adapt to a different set of expectations.
surface culture
The observable and concrete elements of a culture, such as food, dress, and holidays. The visible "leaves" of Hammond's culture tree.
shallow culture
The unspoken rules and norms that govern everyday interactions and ways of being with others (e.g., norms around personal space and nonverbal communication). These are the ways we enact deep-culture beliefs and values.
deep culture
The tacit knowledge and unconscious assumptions that govern our worldview, guiding ethical decision-making and — importantly for this course — shaping the mental models with which we process new information. The "roots" of the culture tree.
socioeconomic status (SES)
A measure of a family's access to resources — particularly financial, but also interpersonal and social. In the U.S., SES strongly shapes children's access to educationally relevant experiences. Reardon's work links rising income inequality to a widening income achievement gap.
income achievement gap
Disparities in educational performance associated with family income. Reardon documents that this gap has widened as differences in family incomes and child-focused spending have grown since the 1950s.
Matthew Effect
The pattern by which early advantages compound over time ("the rich get richer") — e.g., children with larger early vocabularies gain advantages that confer still further learning advantages.
30 million word gap
A hypothesized gap in differential access to language experienced by American children in highly-educated, financially-stable, two-parent families versus children in single-parent, financially-constrained families with low parental education. A widely debated and critiqued finding used in this course to examine hidden cultural assumptions in research.
social construction (of "smartness")
The idea that "smartness" is not a fixed inner trait but is socially situated and produced through what a community values and how it labels learners. What counts as smart depends on shallow-culture practices tied to the dominant deep culture.
teacher expectations
Educators' beliefs about what students can do, which can shape student performance. A review found teachers' negative expectations account for roughly 5–10% of the variance in student achievement (Workman, 2012).
general intelligence (g) / IQ
The historical belief in a fixed, innate level of intelligence ("g"), reported as an intelligence quotient (IQ) and measured by tests like the Stanford-Binet. Long used to place learners in grades or classes; its design embedded — and then appeared to "confirm" — racialized deep-culture beliefs.
eugenics
Policies aiming to limit reproduction to "superior" groups. Some 20th-century intelligence-test results were cited to support eugenics and to restrict immigration — a disturbing legacy of test misuse.
deficit thinking / deficit lens
A framing that interprets learners' differences as deficiencies, locating the "problem" inside the child (e.g., "at-risk," "behind," "lacking," "deviant") rather than in the setting. The course argues against this interpretation.
"at-risk"
A label implying a learner carries risk within themselves, rather than recognizing the risk as emerging from a confrontation between the learner and a setting adapted to others' needs and expectations.
funds of knowledge
"The historically accumulated and culturally developed bodies of knowledge and skills essential for household or individual functioning and well-being" (Moll, Amanti, Neff & Gonzalez, 1992). An asset-based concept: every learner brings resources from home and community.
asset-based view
A stance that treats learners' differences as resources and foundations for learning rather than deficits to remediate. Adopting it often requires a conceptual change for educators.
cultural wealth
The resources for learning and resilience that students from nondominant cultural identities bring to educational settings (Yosso, 2005; Longmire-Avital, 2019).
student-ready colleges
McNair et al.'s (2016) reframing: rather than only producing "college-ready students," institutions should become "student-ready" — adapting to the learners they serve.

07 Practice & discussion

Answer each in your head (or on paper) before revealing. Mix of recall and applied scenarios.

recallName Hammond's three levels of culture and give one example of each.
Surface — observable/concrete (food, dress, holidays). Shallow — unspoken interaction norms (personal space, eye contact, nonverbal cues). Deep — tacit worldview and unconscious assumptions (e.g., beliefs about who is responsible for teaching and learning). Surface = leaves, shallow = enactment of values, deep = roots.
applyA kindergarten teacher is frustrated that a student can't yet name letters but notices the child confidently helps younger classmates and manages classroom routines. Diagnose this through the deficit-vs-asset lens.
Through a deficit lens, the child is "behind" — the gap is inside the child to be fixed. Through an asset / funds-of-knowledge lens, the caregiving and responsibility skills are real, culturally-developed assets (possibly prioritized by the family's deep culture). The mismatch — a classroom built to reward children who already know letters — is the actual "risk," not the child. The corrective is for the school to adapt and build on what the child brings, not for the child alone to change.
recallWhat does the "performance vs. access to resources" graph (children all age 5) show, and which earlier concept does it echo?
Among children of the same age, those with greater access to resources often show higher task performance (e.g., larger vocabularies from high-quality preschool). It echoes the Matthew Effect — early advantages compound, so "the rich get richer."
applyIn Rubin's classroom, Kareem asks "Why not 'Black luxury?'" and is removed from the room. Explain how this single moment illustrates the social construction of smartness.
Kareem's question is genuinely critical and content-related — exactly the higher-order engagement schools claim to value. But the classroom's enacted norms (compliance, low-level tasks, control) signal that this kind of thinking doesn't count as "smart" here; instead it's treated as a disturbance. The interaction tells Kareem and his peers what learning is possible and expected of them. "Smartness" is thus constructed through what the setting values and rewards — tied to the dominant deep culture — not measured as a fixed inner trait.
applyA colleague cites the "30 million word gap" to argue that low-income families simply talk to their kids less, so the kids start school deficient. How would this sequence push back?
Several moves: (1) The finding is heavily debated and critiqued — not settled (Kamenetz, 2018). (2) It can smuggle in cultural assumptions about which kinds of talk "count," privileging dominant-culture norms. (3) Reframing as deficit ("deficient") is exactly the interpretation Moll challenges — all families bring funds of knowledge. (4) Even where resource gaps are real, locating the "risk" inside the child mislabels a mismatch between learners and settings as a flaw in the learners.

Open-ended discussion prompts

  1. Think of a "deep-culture" belief about teaching and learning you hold (e.g., who should be a child's first teacher, autonomy vs. obedience). Where did it come from, and how might it create a mismatch with families whose priorities differ?
  2. Intelligence testing was shaped by — and then appeared to "confirm" — racialized deep-culture beliefs (Gould, Kendi). What assessments in your own context might be quietly encoding cultural assumptions about what counts as "smart"? How would you find out?
  3. What would it concretely look like to build a "student-ready" classroom or program — one that adapts to learners' funds of knowledge — rather than only trying to make learners "ready" for an unchanged institution?

★ Remember this

  • Culture is a "web of meaning" with three levels — surface, shallow, and deep. Deep-culture beliefs (like who owns teaching) are invisible but powerful, and mismatches with school norms can harm learning.
  • All families want similar outcomes (LeVine); they differ in how they prioritize them. There's huge variability within any group — never read research patterns as rules about individuals.
  • Resources (SES) shape opportunity. At the same age, more resources often track with higher performance (Matthew Effect) — but read findings like the "30 million word gap" critically for hidden cultural assumptions.
  • "Smartness" is socially constructed. What counts as smart reflects what the dominant culture values; teacher expectations and a legacy of biased intelligence testing actively shape who gets labeled — and supported.
  • Difference ≠ deficiency. Relocate the "risk" from inside the child to the mismatch between learner and setting.
  • Lead with assets. Every learner has funds of knowledge and cultural wealth — the goal is schools that adapt to children, not children who must conform to schools.
