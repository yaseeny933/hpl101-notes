Romania's Abandoned Children

Charles Nelson (HGSE) · Romanian orphanages, 1980s–90s

4.1.1
Context

Infants raised in mid-century Romanian orphanages were institutionalized but given no chance to bond with any consistent caregiving adult.

What happened

Nelson followed these children and found atypical development, especially in relating to others: many showed indiscriminate social behavior (hugging or leaping into strangers' arms) and struggled to initiate play or respond to peers, and teachers rated them as less socially skillful. Yet children placed into foster families by about 22 months could still form warm attachments — a window, not a verdict.

Concept it illustrates

Attachment — the bond formed through reliable, responsive caregiving (the "serve and return" exchange) is the foundation for healthy social-emotional development. Its absence carries long-lasting consequences, but early intervention can still restore it.

Takeaway for practice

Responsive, reliable relationships are not a "nice to have" — they are developmental infrastructure. Build in consistent, attentive adult contact early, and treat the early years as a window where it matters most.

Simple Interactions

Junlei Li (HGSE) & colleagues · a descriptive observation tool

4.1.1
Context

Li's team wanted a way to spot the "active ingredient" in relationships across many settings — classrooms, museums, makerspaces, playgrounds — and across cultures, without imposing one culture's norms of "good" interaction.

What happened

They built a framework naming four dimensions of human interaction — Connection, Reciprocity, Inclusion, and Opportunity to Grow — with illustrations showing modes labeled X, Y, Z (not 1–2–3) so observers describe before they judge. It was deliberately built as a descriptive, not evaluative, tool so that closeness expressed in many cultural styles (smiling or quietly focused) all count.

Concept it illustrates

Relationships as the active ingredient in learning, and culturally inclusive observation. "Opportunity to Grow" is literally guidance in the Vygotskian ZPD with scaffolding that fades.

Takeaway for practice

Audit everyday adult–child moments for connection, reciprocity, inclusion, and opportunity to grow. Describe what you see before grading it — and don't mistake one culture's warmth style for the only valid one.

The "Get to Know You" Exercise

Gehlbach et al. (2016) · teacher–student relationships study

4.1.2
Context

Teacher–student relationships (TSRs) are among the strongest predictors of achievement (Hattie), but trust is hard to build when teachers and students lack common ground.

What happened

Researchers ran a simple intervention surfacing a handful of preferences that a teacher and student actually shared. The result: more positive interactions and better student grades. The proposed mechanism — surfacing commonalities helped both sides build a more trusting relationship.

Concept it illustrates

Trust as the basis of teacher–student relationships. Trust is earned and conditional, and shared ground (emotional support, organization, instructional support) is what learners need to take risks and engage.

Takeaway for practice

Small, deliberate relationship-building beats hoping rapport "just happens." Find and name genuine common ground early; it pays off in engagement and outcomes.

Building a Psychologically Safe Workplace

Amy Edmondson (HBS) · research on team effectiveness

4.1.4
Context

Adults keep learning through relationships at work. One condition makes work teams able to learn: whether members feel safe taking interpersonal risks.

What happened

Edmondson defines psychological safety as the shared belief that a team is safe for risk-taking. Safe teams let members make mistakes in the service of learning, ask for help, be different, and feel included even when they fail. The enabling conditions are concrete: leaders model humility and admit mistakes, act as coaches, avoid domineering, while members seek help and minimize status differences — adopting a learning stance over an execution stance.

Concept it illustrates

Psychological safety as the relational condition for adult learning and collaboration — the grown-up parallel to the safety attachment gives a child.

Takeaway for practice

If you lead any group, deliberately model fallibility and reward help-seeking. "Safe" doesn't mean comfortable — it means people aren't punished for the risks that learning requires.

The Identity Project

Adriana Umaña-Taylor (HGSE) & colleagues · school-based ERI curriculum

4.2.3
Context

Ethnic–racial identity (ERI) develops across family, peers, school, and society. Over 75% of U.S. youth of color report identity-based discrimination, with documented harms to achievement and well-being.

What happened

The Identity Project is an evidence-based, universal health-promotion curriculum for middle and high schoolers that deliberately fosters ERI exploration and resolution. It is being tested in ethnically–racially diverse U.S. schools and has been adapted for seventh graders in Berlin, Germany (reframed around cultural identity). Across studies, higher ERI achievement is tied to better academic outcomes and serves as both a promotive and a protective factor — e.g., buffering Latinx adolescents from the effects of online racial discrimination.

Concept it illustrates

ERI as a promotive and protective factor (risk-and-resilience framework). A secure, explored ethnic–racial identity compensates for adversity and reduces the harm of discrimination.

Takeaway for practice

Identity work is not a distraction from achievement — it supports it. Give learners structured chances to explore and make sense of who they are; it builds resilience that shows up academically.

Stereotype Threat

Claude Steele & Joshua Aronson (1995) · the founding experiments

4.3.2
Context

Why do groups of equal measured ability perform differently on the same test? Steele and Aronson recruited African-American and White students with similar verbal SAT scores.

What happened

When the test was framed as a measure of verbal/reasoning ability, African-American students underperformed despite equal capability; in parallel work, women underperformed men on math when told the test measured math ability. Follow-ups showed the effect lives in the conditions, not ability: telling women a test was "designed to eliminate gender differences" erased the gap; invoking a growth mindset (intelligence is malleable; struggle is normal) also removed it.

Concept it illustrates

Stereotype threat — an internalized expectation that depresses performance when a negative group stereotype is made salient under evaluation, especially when one is in the minority in the room.

Takeaway for practice

Reduce threat by reframing evaluative tasks, lowering identity salience, and teaching that struggle reflects the situation — not a deficiency. The fix is in the environment, not the learner.

Ethnic Studies as "Precious Knowledge"

Tucson MAS program · & Dee & Penner (2017), San Francisco USD

4.3.4
Context

Ethnic studies re-centers a curriculum long focused on dominant subgroups, teaching counter-narratives that build critical consciousness about power and privilege.

What happened

Tucson, Arizona's Mexican-American Studies (MAS / la Raza Studies) program taught Indigenous Xicanx intellectual traditions, art, and literature — knowledge students and teachers called "precious." It was legally banned, then reinstated after court battles. On outcomes: Dee & Penner exploited a natural experiment in San Francisco USD, where students below a GPA cutoff were assigned to an ethnic-studies course and compared to barely-above-cutoff peers. Participation raised attendance by 21 percentage points, ninth-grade GPA by 1.4 points, and credits earned by 23.

Concept it illustrates

Culturally sustaining pedagogy / culturally responsive & relevant education (CRRE) — centering students' identities and cultural knowledge, plus the documented academic payoff of doing so well.

Takeaway for practice

Curriculum that honors students' cultures and histories is not only affirming — when well-implemented it can measurably lift attendance, grades, and engagement.

Family–School Partnership at Stanton Elementary

Mapp & Kuttner (2013) · Washington, DC turnaround school

4.4.4
Context

In 2010 Stanton Elementary, a struggling DC charter, came under new leadership. Parents were disconnected; staff and families were "battling one another." Early instructional and culture fixes alone didn't move performance.

What happened

In 2011 the school added a deliberate family–school partnership: (1) teacher home visits to students' families, and (2) teams of teachers and caregivers meeting to support students collaboratively. By the end of the 2012 school year, performance began improving — illustrating the "opportunity conditions" of Mapp & Kuttner's dual-capacity-building framework.

Concept it illustrates

Family and community engagement via dual capacity-building — building schools' capacity to serve families and families' capacity to engage schools. Belonging and achievement require partnering with communities, not bypassing them.

Takeaway for practice

Open the door both ways: go to families (home visits) and bring them in as genuine partners in decisions. Engagement is a relationship to build, not a form to send home.

★ Apply it

Match each new situation to the case or concept it most resembles. Reveal to check.

A manager notices her team has gone quiet in meetings. No one admits when a project is off-track, and junior staff never ask questions in front of senior colleagues. She wants people to flag problems early. Which Module 4 case and concept applies, and what should she do?

Case: Edmondson's Building a Psychologically Safe Workplace (4.1.4). The silence and the status-driven reluctance to ask questions are classic low-psychological-safety signals.

Move: She should model humility and admit her own mistakes, act as a coach rather than an authority, minimize status differences, and reward help-seeking — shifting the team from an "execution" stance to a "learning" stance.

A capable student of color consistently scores lower on high-stakes timed exams than her coursework predicts. She is often the only person from her background in the testing room, and she describes second-guessing answers under pressure. What is likely happening, and what does the research suggest?

Case: Steele & Aronson's Stereotype Threat (4.3.2). Evaluation + identity salience + being in the minority is exactly the condition that evokes it; second-guessing correct answers is a documented behavior.

Move: Reframe the test instructions (e.g., as fair across groups), reduce identity salience in the setting, and invoke a growth mindset — teaching that difficulty reflects the task and context, not her ability. The fix targets the environment, not the learner.

A district wants to lift achievement for marginalized students and is debating whether a course built around students' own cultural histories is "a distraction from academics." What evidence from Module 4 speaks to this, and what concept is at stake?

Case: Ethnic Studies as "Precious Knowledge" (4.3.4) — the Tucson MAS program plus Dee & Penner's San Francisco study, which found ethnic-studies participation raised attendance (+21 pts), GPA (+1.4), and credits earned (+23).

Concept: Culturally sustaining / culturally responsive & relevant education. Centering students' identities is not a trade-off against achievement; well-implemented, it can raise it.

A new principal at a low-performing school improves instruction and discipline, but scores barely move. Parents feel shut out and staff and families distrust one another. Which case should guide the next step, and what is the underlying framework?

Case: Stanton Elementary (4.4.4). Instruction-and-culture fixes alone weren't enough there either — performance moved only after a family–school partnership was added.

Move & framework: Add structured engagement — teacher home visits and teacher–caregiver teams — guided by Mapp & Kuttner's dual capacity-building framework, which builds the school's capacity to serve families and families' capacity to engage the school.

★ The dossier in one glance

  • Relationships are infrastructure. Romania's children show attachment's stakes; Simple Interactions names its active ingredients (connection, reciprocity, inclusion, opportunity to grow).
  • Trust and safety let people take risks. The get-to-know-you study builds teacher–student trust; Edmondson shows psychological safety doing the same for adult teams.
  • Identity is a learning resource. The Identity Project makes ERI a promotive and protective factor for diverse learners.
  • The environment, not the learner, is the lever. Stereotype threat proves equal ability can yield unequal scores — and that reframing and growth mindset close the gap.
  • Centering culture pays off. Tucson MAS + Dee & Penner show culturally sustaining curriculum raising real outcomes.
  • Partner with families. Stanton Elementary moved only when home visits and caregiver teams built capacity on both sides.
