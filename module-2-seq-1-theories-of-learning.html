HPL101 · Module 2 · Sequence 1

Theories of Learning

Four lenses on the same lesson. Each brings part of learning into focus — and blurs the rest. Knowing all four lets you diagnose, and re-frame, any classroom.

behaviorism cognitivism constructivism · constructionism critical consciousness

01 Four lenses on learning

A theory of learning is a lens: it brings certain aspects of how we grow and change into sharp view, while letting others recede or blur. This sequence examines four theories that shaped a century of education — each illustrated through one teacher, Nyan, planning the same lesson on the 1942–45 U.S. internment of Japanese Americans.

The theories are not mutually exclusive, and there is no single "right" one. Each clarifies some things while deemphasizing others. The educator's job is to recognize the implicit theory behind their choices — and to switch lenses when another would better serve the learner.

Black box → open the mind → builds knowledge → transforms society. The lens shifts from what's outside the learner to what's inside, then to what the learner makes and the society they're in.

02 Behaviorism — Unit 2.1.1

the black box

Behaviorism holds that behaviors are the result of reflexes and consequences — responses to environmental stimuli plus an individual's history of past rewards and punishments. Behaviors are learned, not innate; environment and experience are the key drivers.

Crucially, it does not care what happens "inside the head." The mind is treated as a black box — what's inside is unknown, but that's fine, because to produce learning we only need to manage the observable inputs (stimuli) and outputs (behavior).

Learning happens through conditioning

Classical
Learning by association, no reward or punishment. Pavlov's bell becomes linked to feeding; the response is involuntary. Two stimuli (one neutral, one potent) are paired until the neutral one alone provokes the response.
Operant
A voluntary behavior (the "operant") is emitted, then reinforced or punished, changing how likely it is to recur. B.F. Skinner's domain — and the heart of behaviorist teaching: reinforce desired behaviors.
Bottom-up
Both are bottom-up, associative learning: the stimulus drives a reflexive response; no cognition or reflection is brought in. Related to rote learning (memorization by repetition).
Modern echo
Reinforcement learning in AI (e.g. how ChatGPT trains) is bottom-up too — it predicts word associations and is rewarded/punished for accuracy, with no "understanding," planning, or reasoning.
stimulus → response reinforcement / punishment immediate vs. delayed feedback grades & stars as reinforcers

Nyan, behaviorist: graded quizzes (points reinforce study), a flashcard game (stars reward, losing stars punishes) with immediate feedback, and hand-written comments for top scorers (another reward). The ungraded pre-test is deliberately neither reinforced nor punished.

03 Cognitivism — Unit 2.1.2

open the mind

Emerging mid-20th century partly in reaction to behaviorism, cognitivism focuses on how information is received, organized, stored, and retrieved by the mind. Like behaviorists, cognitivists agree the environment shapes learning — but the mind is absolutely not a black box. The learner is active: they plan, set goals, rehearse, store, and relate new information to what they already know. Thoughts, beliefs, attitudes, and values all matter.

Learning = changing or elaborating mental representations and structures. Knowledge lives in schemas — organized webs of ideas, meanings, and the relationships among them. Learning wires a new node into the web or rebuilds the web; it isn't just adding an isolated fact.

Intuitive theories — abstract first

Beyond schemas, learners hold intuitive theories: abstract, causal frameworks used to predict, explain, reason about, and intervene on the world — like scientists' theories of nature. Contrary to the assumption that knowledge goes concrete → abstract, even very young children form abstract causal theories early, sometimes before the concrete facts.

Goals first
Infants expect agents to pursue goals via the easiest path — before learning customs like handshakes.
Rules first
Toddlers grasp abstract syntax before irregulars — hence "I goed" instead of "I went."
"Why?"
Frank Keil (Wonder): kids constantly ask explanatory "why" questions. Delaying causal science to grades 6–8 underestimates them.
schemas assimilation / accommodation intuitive theories active learner

Nyan, cognitivist: a discussion that surfaces existing beliefs (activating prior schemas), asking whether the internment idea applies to Native American reservations (stretching/rebuilding a schema across contexts), and concept maps that make the web of relationships visible.

04 Constructivism & Constructionism — Unit 2.1.3

build knowledge

Generally a branch of cognitivism, but it treats the world as less external and objective. Constructivists argue learning is creating meaning from experience — we create knowledge rather than acquire it. Learners build internal representations from their own experiences and interactions, always open to revision. John Dewey is its philosophical founder: he rejected drill and rote in favor of "directed living" — real-world activity that demands thinking, from which "learning naturally results." It's compatible with both Piagetian and Vygotskian views (deep structures built over time, through social interaction).

Theory theory
Researchers applying constructivism to intuitive theories. The theories are defeasible — flexibly revised from observation, like scientific inquiry. This straddles cognitivism and constructivism.
Inductive inference
Children also learn by guessing general rules from a few observations, guided by prior knowledge ("inductive biases"). E.g. infants expect a human hand (but not a robotic claw) to keep pursuing a toy that has moved — they attribute goals to agents. This contrasts with behaviorism's bottom-up-only account.
Constructionism
"Learning through making." Seymour Papert: we learn best by doing, and better still by talking and thinking about what we made — "especially felicitously" when consciously building a public entity. Project-based learning and tools/artifacts/media externalize understanding.

The sibling difference: constructivists care about learning from any experience and reflection on it; constructionists emphasize actually making personally meaningful, often public, things. Both prize learner agency and both see learning as deeply social.

constructivism constructionism inductive inference Dewey · Papert learner agency

Nyan, constructivist/constructionist: group interviews with internment survivors (collaborative, inquiry-driven meaning-making), a first-person journal reflection (building internal representations), and a student-made podcast or video (building knowledge through making — with the choice itself giving agency).

05 Critical Consciousness & Synthesis — Unit 2.1.4

transform society

This lens originates not in a view of cognition but in a view of social reality: what we learn, and how, is shaped by the maintenance of some groups' power. Beyond describing society's rules and norms, critically conscious thinkers expose how the "normal" advantages the powerful at others' expense. Brazilian educator Paulo Freire advanced this framework in the 1970s. Critically conscious learners actively name and resist injustice — even questioning "WEIRD" science (Module 1) is a small act of it.

Double consciousness

W.E.B. Du Bois (1903): the experience of seeing oneself through both one's own authentic lived experience and the "jarring, denigrating view" of dominant groups. (Harvard gave Du Bois — among its most influential graduates — only partial credit for his Fisk HBCU degree.) The dominant story protects the powerful but is harmfully inaccurate and ignores the lived reality of the subordinated. Scholars extend it to gender, sexuality, class, citizenship, religion, and more — e.g. Thai kathoey identities and Afro-Brazilian "double linguistic consciousness."

critical consciousness conscientização (Freire) double consciousness (Du Bois) power & oppression

Nyan, critical: researching whether internment is isolated or echoed today, contrasting an official military history with survivor-centered accounts (whose perspectives get told?), and connecting to modern family separation at the border and the activists resisting it.

Thinking across theories: none is "the right one." Each clarifies some of learning and obscures the rest, so we risk an incomplete picture if we don't put them in dialogue. Use them to diagnose the assumptions behind a learning experience and to re-frame it with an alternative when that better serves your goals.

07 Glossary — quiz yourself first

Every key term from the four units' glossaries. Try to define each before you reveal it.

behaviorism
A theory of learning suggesting that people's behaviors are responses to environmental stimuli, and thus can be shaped by conditioning. Treats the mind as a "black box" — only inputs and outputs matter.
conditioning
The process of a person associating certain stimuli with particular behaviors.
classical conditioning
Learning that occurs from repeated pairing of two stimuli, one neutral and the other biologically potent and able to provoke a response. Eventually the neutral stimulus alone provokes the same response as the potent stimulus. Pavlov's bell → salivation. Association only; no reward or punishment.
operant conditioning
Learning in which the strength of a behavior is reinforced through reward (or omission of reward) or through punishment. Skinner's domain — the basis of behaviorist teaching.
bottom-up, associative learning
A way of learning by forming connections based on repeated experiences; starts from basic sensory information but can build to more complex representations. The stimulus drives the response; no cognition or reflection is brought in.
rote learning
A memorization technique using repetitions, often without full understanding of the content or deployment of reflective, "top-down" engagement with the material.
reinforcement learning
A type of learning where a learner (or algorithm, in machine learning) learns to make decisions by receiving rewards or penalties for its actions. How models like ChatGPT train — bottom-up, no real "understanding."
cognitivism
A theory positing that learning is an active mental process of changing mental representations and structures. The mind is absolutely not a black box.
schemas
Organized mental systems of information, ideas, meanings, conditions, and the relationships among these components. Learning happens by changing or elaborating them.
intuitive theory
An abstract, causal mental framework that supports prediction, explanation, reasoning, and exploration. "I goed" — a child applies an abstract rule before learning the irregular exception.
constructivism
A theory that posits learning is a process of creating meaning from experience — building and modifying internal representations of knowledge based on one's individual experiences and interactions.
constructionism
A theory that people learn best when engaged in making tangible objects. Papert's "learning through making" — often building a public entity.
inductive inference
A type of reasoning or learning where one makes a general guess or prediction based on a few specific observations or examples. Depends on prior "inductive biases" shaping how the learner generalizes.
critical consciousness
The view that what and how we learn are shaped by the systems of power and oppression within society. Freire's framework: name and resist injustice (conscientização).
double consciousness
W. E. B. Du Bois's description of the experience of members of oppressed social groups seeing the world through both their own authentic lived experiences and simultaneously through the jarring, denigrating view of socially dominant groups.

08 Practice & Discussion

Test yourself first, then reveal each answer. The discussion prompts have no single right answer — they're for seminar talk and your own teaching journal.

Test yourself

1. What does it mean that behaviorism treats the mind as a "black box," and why is that acceptable to a behaviorist?
It means behaviorism is not concerned with what goes on inside the learner's head — thoughts, beliefs, values, and reasoning are treated as unknown and unnecessary to examine. That's fine to a behaviorist because, to produce learning, you only need to manage the observable inputs (stimuli) and outputs (behavior). What's "inside" can stay unknown.
2. Classical vs. operant conditioning — what's the key difference?
Classical conditioning is learning by association alone, with no reward or punishment; the response is involuntary (Pavlov's bell paired with feeding until the bell alone triggers salivation). Operant conditioning involves a voluntary behavior (the "operant") that is then reinforced or punished, changing how likely it is to recur (Skinner's domain). Both are "bottom-up, associative learning" — but only operant conditioning hinges on consequences, which is why it's central to behaviorist teaching.
3. Which theory is this? Ms. Okafor begins a unit by leading a class discussion that surfaces what students already believe about when imprisoning people is just or unjust, then later asks whether that belief applies to Native American reservations, and has students draw concept maps of the connections. Which of the four theories does this most reflect, and why?
Cognitivism. The moves all target the inside of the learner's mind: surfacing prior beliefs activates existing schemas, transferring the idea to a new context (reservations) stretches and rebuilds those schemas, and concept maps make the web of relationships among concepts visible. The mind is treated as absolutely not a black box; the learner is an active processor relating new information to what they already know. (Note: a self-made concept map can also lean constructivist — but here the emphasis is on reshaping mental structures.)
4. Which theory is this? Mr. Diallo has students compare a traditional military historian's policy analysis of the internment with a survivor-centered account, asks whether internment is an isolated event or echoed today, and connects it to present-day family separation at the border and the activists resisting it. Which theory is this, and why?
Critical consciousness. The lesson centers on power and whose story gets told: contrasting the official history with subordinated voices exposes how learning is shaped by groups maintaining power, and linking to family separation plus activist resistance invites students to name and resist injustice. Imagining how undocumented immigrants are seen by those enacting the policies is an exercise in double consciousness (Du Bois); the framework traces to Paulo Freire.
5. Constructivism and constructionism are siblings — what's the difference in emphasis, and what do they share?
Both hold that we create knowledge rather than acquire it, both prize learner agency, and both see learning as deeply social. The difference is emphasis: constructivists care about learning from any experience and reflection on it; constructionists (Papert's "learning through making") emphasize actually making personally meaningful, often public, things. Dewey is the philosophical founder of constructivism ("directed living"); Papert is credited with constructionism — we learn by doing, and better still by talking and thinking about what we made.
6. A young child says "I goed to the park." Which theory best explains this, and what does it illustrate about how knowledge develops?
Cognitivism — specifically the idea of intuitive theories. The child has grasped an abstract syntax rule (add "-ed" for past tense) before memorizing the concrete irregular exception ("went"). This overturns the lay assumption that knowledge always goes concrete → abstract: even very young children form abstract, causal frameworks early — sometimes before the concrete facts. Underestimating this (e.g., delaying causal science to grades 6–8, per Frank Keil) sells learners short.

Discussion prompts

A. Pick a single lesson or unit you actually teach (or might teach). Sketch how you would design it through each of the four lenses in turn. Which lens is your default? Which feels most foreign — and what would you gain, or risk losing, by deliberately switching to it?
B. The sequence insists there is "no single right" theory and that the theories are "not mutually exclusive." Do you find that fully convincing, or are there places where the four lenses genuinely conflict (e.g., behaviorist reinforcement vs. constructivist agency, or cognitivist schema-building vs. critical consciousness's focus on power)? Argue for where you'd draw the lines.
C. Critical consciousness asks "whose power?" of any learning experience. Turn that lens on a learning environment you know well — a course, a curriculum, a standardized exam, or even this study sequence itself. Whose perspectives are centered, whose recede, and what would a more critically conscious redesign change?

★ Remember this

  • Four lenses, one lesson. Behaviorism, cognitivism, constructivism, and critical consciousness each foreground different parts of learning — none is "the right one."
  • The spectrum runs outside → inside → made → social. Black box (behavior) → open mind (schemas) → builds knowledge (meaning & making) → transforms society (power).
  • Behaviorism = stimulus & response. Learning by conditioning (classical = association; operant = reward/punishment); the mind is ignored.
  • Cognitivism & constructivism open and build the mind. Cognitivists reshape schemas; constructivists/constructionists say we create knowledge — especially by making things (Dewey, Papert).
  • Critical consciousness asks "whose power?" Freire and Du Bois: learning means seeing and resisting how the "normal" advantages some at others' expense (double consciousness).
  • Switch lenses on purpose. Use the theories to diagnose the assumptions behind a learning experience and re-frame it when another lens serves learners better.
