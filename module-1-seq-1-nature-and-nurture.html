Nature & Nurture

It was never "either/or." Development is genes and experience, continuously intertwined across a whole lifespan.

development nature ⇄ nurture epigenetics sensitive periods

01 The core idea of the sequence

"Development" is the process of change over an organism's life toward a more complex organism — but how we define it is loaded with cultural values. This sequence retires the old nature-vs-nurture either/or and replaces it with a richer, biologically grounded picture.

The big move: stop asking "nature OR nurture?" Human development is a product of both genes and experience, and modern biology (epigenetics) shows they are actively and continuously intertwined. The timing of experience also matters — there are sensitive periods when experience has outsized, lasting effects — yet the brain stays plastic and "adult" never means "finished growing."
  • 1.1.1 — What we even mean by "development" is culturally and historically shaped; take a critical stance toward whose research defines it.
  • 1.1.2 — The classic debate runs from pure heredity to the "blank slate" (tabula rasa); educators' beliefs carry hidden assumptions.
  • 1.1.3 — Science says it's both: epigenetics is the mechanism by which environment switches genes on and off.
  • 1.1.4 — Timing matters: critical vs. sensitive periods — but stay skeptical of overgeneralized claims about people.

02 1.1.1 · Thinking about "Development"

Words like learn and develop are not neutral — they're "laden with underlying values and interpretations." Dr. Barry Brazelton's relational, child-centered view of the newborn is a contemporary, Western conception, not a universal truth.

Key moves

  • Ideas about childhood are rooted in specific intellectual, social, and cultural contexts; until the last century, prevailing views of childhood were very different.
  • We still debate what makes childhood/adolescence distinct from adulthood, what's biology vs. experience, and what that means for teaching.
  • The Scientist in the Crib argues even very young children have innate motivation to learn by exploring — purposeful, like little adults.
  • Some concepts (germs, deities) can only be learned from other people; even grade-by-age schooling is debated (mixed-age grouping may sometimes be better).
  • Development is lifelong — "adult" does not mean "finished changing."
Take a critical stance. Most research evidence has been shaped by European and North American academic traditions. Theories rarely represent holistic, global views of development, so evaluate your sources critically and watch for under- or misrepresented perspectives.

03 1.1.2 · Perspectives on Nature & Nurture

When does nature vs. nurture exert the greater influence? The debate stretches between two extremes:

Nature heredity

Most of who we become is shaped by heredity — the genes and bodies we're born with.

Extreme view: personhood is largely fixed at birth.

Nurture tabula rasa

Each of us is born a "blank slate" (tabula rasa) that fills in through stimuli and varied experience in the world.

Extreme view: who we become is written entirely by environment.

Beliefs carry hidden assumptions

Educators' opinions about biology vs. environment smuggle in assumptions that shape practice. The unit surfaces them:

"Educated parents → kids do well because of inherited capacity."Assumption Intelligence is primarily determined by heredity.
"Stressful early home → unlikely to excel academically."Assumption Early exposure to stress influences cognitive development.
"A teacher who expects all students can succeed is the top predictor."Assumption Teacher expectations matter more than pedagogical skill for student growth.
"Inequitable access by race/income harms outcomes more than bad teaching."Assumption Institutional factors outweigh interpersonal ones in disadvantaging groups.
"We need tracks/GATE or too many students aren't at the right level."Assumption Some students simply can't learn as fast — intelligence is relatively fixed.

↳ Takeaway: there's a full array of views. The essential skill is to think critically about your own stance and its implications for working with developing learners.

04 1.1.3 · Genetics, Environment & Epigenetics

What does science actually say? Recent biology upended last century's assumptions. Genes and experience aren't separate inputs — they are actively and continuously intertwined, and the field studying that intersection is epigenetics.

The genetic code is less like a fixed formula and more like a symphonic score — interpreted differently by every conductor and orchestra. The environment is the conductor: it decides how and when each part is played. The centrality of both genes and experience is common to us all; the details of the interaction are unique to each person.

Environment doesn't rewrite the DNA letters — it changes which genes are expressed, shaping development.

What epigenetics tells us (Check-for-Understanding)

  • There is a biological mechanism by which environment influences biology — epigenetics studies that interaction.
  • Positive and negative environments can have a lasting impact on which genes are turned on and off.
  • Triggers include social experiences, diet, chemical exposure, and medication — not just one kind of factor.
  • Crucially, it does not change the genetic code itself — it modifies gene expression.
  • Most epigenetic marks are erased, but some may be passed to subsequent generations.

05 1.1.4 · Brain Development & "Sensitive Periods"

Are there times when experience has a pronounced influence? In the animal kingdom, goslings imprint during a rigid critical period — miss it and they won't imprint under any conditions. Humans are different: we attach rather than imprint, over a longer, fuzzier window.

Critical vs. sensitive period

Critical periodSensitive period
The windowDefined beginning & end — narrow, rigidFuzzy beginning & end — broader, looser
If experience is missedCapacity may never develop normallySkill can still develop later, but with possible lasting impairment
ExampleFirst-language exposure; losing ability to hear non-native sound contrasts (English baby loses Mandarin tones)Early childhood as the easier window for language & many other domains
Animal analogyGoslings imprinting in hoursHumans' extended early childhood

The neuroscience

  • In the first years, as many as a million new neural connections form per second; basic brain architecture takes shape early.
  • Unused connections are "pruned," making circuits more efficient — a neurological basis for why timing of experience matters.
  • Brains keep changing into adulthood, but later experience tends to strengthen/weaken existing connections rather than build millions of new ones — more integrated, efficient networks.
  • Because of lifelong plasticity, many developmentalists now question hard "critical periods" — and even doubt some "sensitive periods." You can learn an additional language at any age, given the right conditions.
  • People differ not just in genes and environments but in susceptibility to those environmental and experiential effects.
Evidence & ethics. Much of what we know comes from extreme cases of childhood deprivation (Robson, 2002). The resilience of deprived children given later care suggests early childhood is better called a "sensitive" than a "critical" period for language & social development. Throughout, keep an active, healthy skepticism toward claims that overgeneralize about complex, diverse humans.

06 Glossary — quiz yourself first

Try to define each term before you reveal it.

Piaget
Theorist who proposed that development consists of successive, qualitatively different stages through which individuals pass. Stage theory: each stage is a distinct kind of thinking, not just "more" of the previous one.
development
The process of change that occurs during an organism's life to produce a more complex organism. And remember — it's lifelong; "adult" doesn't mean "finished."
nature
The contribution of heredity — the genes and bodies we're born with — to who we become. The extreme "nature" position holds that most of our personhood is shaped by heredity. One end of the long-standing nature/nurture debate.
nurture
The contribution of environment and experience to development. The extreme "nurture" position is the tabula rasa: we are born blank slates filled in by stimuli and experience. The opposite pole from "nature" — though science now says it's both.
tabula rasa
"Blank slate" — the view that a person is born without built-in content and is wholly filled in by experience and the stimuli they encounter in the world. The extreme nurture stance.
epigenetics
The study of changes in organisms caused by environmentally induced modification of gene expression, rather than alteration of the genetic code itself. The environment turns genes "on" and "off" — the score stays the same, the performance changes.
sensitive period
A period during which the presence or absence of certain forms of experience have particularly large consequences for development. Fuzzy edges: skills can still develop afterward, but missing the window can leave lasting impairment.
critical period
A period of time in which a specific event or stimulus must be encountered for normal development to occur. Narrow and rigid — like goslings imprinting, or first-language exposure in humans.

★ Remember this

  • Not either/or. Development is genes and experience, continuously intertwined — and it lasts a lifetime.
  • "Development" is culturally loaded. Most evidence comes from Western traditions; take a critical stance on whose view defines it.
  • Beliefs hide assumptions. Educators' nature/nurture opinions carry assumptions (e.g. "intelligence is fixed") that shape practice.
  • Epigenetics is the mechanism. Environment switches genes on/off without changing the code; effects can be lasting and sometimes heritable.
  • Timing matters. Critical periods are narrow and rigid; sensitive periods are broad and fuzzy. The brain stays plastic — you can learn a second language at any age.
  • Stay skeptical. Resist overgeneralized claims; people differ in genes, environments, and their susceptibility to experience.
